Monday, July 6, 2026 · North America focus
Morning brief: Energy capital is the story of the week: National Grid Ventures put $1.75B behind Chevron/GE Vernova's 2.67 GW gas campus for Microsoft in Texas, Brookfield pushed its Bloom Energy fuel-cell commitment to $25B, and Alberta's Greenlight Electricity Centre reached a $4.6B final investment decision for a 932 MW plant tied to a hyperscale customer. 1

National Grid Ventures invests $1.75B in Joulent to build 2.67 GW gas campus for Microsoft in Texas

EnergyFinance

National Grid's commercial arm agreed to invest $1.75 billion for a 35% stake in power developer Joulent, backing "Project Kilby" — a 2.67 GW natural-gas generation campus in Reeves County, Texas, being built with Chevron subsidiary Energy Forge and GE Vernova to supply a Microsoft-operated data centre campus (July 3).

Why it matters: A major grid operator is now buying directly into behind-the-meter generation for hyperscalers — dedicated on-site gas is becoming core utility strategy, not a workaround for interconnection queues.

Digital Realty buys out Blackstone's $3.5B stake in three Northern Virginia data centres

FinanceConstruction

Digital Realty will pay $1.2 billion in cash and $2.3 billion in shares to acquire Blackstone-managed funds' interests in three fully leased Northern Virginia facilities — two 96 MW sites in Manassas and one 96 MW site in Sterling — valuing the portfolio at $7.8 billion including debt and capex (June 29-30).

Why it matters: One of the largest single-market data centre buyouts this year shows REITs consolidating control in the world's biggest data centre hub even as financing costs and community pushback rise around it.

Pembina and partners reach $4.6B final investment decision on Alberta gas plant for data centre

CanadaEnergy

Pembina Pipeline, Morgan Stanley Infrastructure Partners, and Kineticor Asset Management have taken final investment decision on the Greenlight Electricity Centre, a 932 MW natural gas plant in Alberta's Industrial Heartland north of Edmonton built to power a data centre customer, at a cost of $4.6 billion (July 2, construction starting Q3 2026).

Why it matters: Alberta's bring-your-own-generation model — data centres paying for dedicated power and related infrastructure rather than burdening the grid — is now moving from policy to poured concrete.

9

Samsung Heavy Industries targets 2028 commercialization of floating data centres

Tech

Samsung Heavy Industries says it will complete commercialization of a floating data centre (FDC) barge by Q2 2028, after securing Approval in Principle from the American Bureau of Shipping and Lloyd's Register for a 50 MW design (July 6). Its Dallas-based partner Mousterian is pitching the concept as a way to deliver 1.5 GW of capacity within 36 months by sidestepping land and grid constraints.

Why it matters: If speed-to-power keeps being the industry's binding constraint, sea-based capacity that skips permitting and interconnection queues could become a real release valve for North American demand — though scale and reliability doubts remain.

Texas Governor Abbott calls for data centres to be banned in rural areas

Policy

Gov. Greg Abbott — historically one of the industry's strongest supporters — has called for data centres to be banned in rural Texas (July 3). Why it matters: When the governor of the fastest-growing data centre state turns against rural siting, political risk has risen sharply across the entire ERCOT market.

18

Virginia's first-ever data centre power tax takes effect

PolicyEnergy

Virginia's new consumption tax of $0.011/kWh on all electricity used by data centres took effect July 1, projected to raise roughly $600 million annually for the state's general fund, while preserving the industry's existing sales-tax exemption.

Why it matters: The world's largest data centre market has now put a direct price on power consumption — expect other jurisdictions, including Canadian provinces, to study the model closely.

19

Nvidia's Rubin design claims near-zero water use with 100% liquid cooling

TechEnergy

Nvidia's Rubin-generation reference design cools every chip and networking component with liquid in a closed loop — no fans, no cooling towers — running coolant at up to 45°C so many climates can skip mechanical chillers entirely (announced June 22, still rippling through supplier stocks in late June). Nvidia says the design cuts water use toward zero and cooling-related energy use by up to 40%.

Why it matters: Water has become as contentious a constraint as electricity for new builds; a reference design from the industry's dominant chipmaker could reset the baseline for every new North American AI campus.

FuelCell Energy signs deal for up to 380 MW of clean power for data centres

Energy

FuelCell Energy and developer Fit Energy announced a strategic agreement covering up to 380 MW of carbonate fuel cell capacity across four phases, with an initial 30 MW deposit and deliveries beginning later this year (announced June 24).

Why it matters: Another fuel-cell order of scale confirms distributed, on-site generation is becoming a standard part of the data centre power stack alongside gas and nuclear, not just a niche pilot technology.
