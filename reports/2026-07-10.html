Morning brief: Power and policy dominate today. FERC issued historic show-cause orders forcing all six major US grid operators to justify or rewrite their rules for connecting gigawatt-scale data centres, with intervention deadlines landing today, while Virginia's first-in-the-nation data centre electricity tax is now collecting 1.1¢/kWh. 1

FERC orders all six US grid operators to justify or rewrite data centre interconnection rules

EnergyPolicy

The Federal Energy Regulatory Commission issued Section 206 show-cause orders to PJM, MISO, SPP, CAISO, ISO-NE and NYISO, directing each to either prove its current large-load interconnection tariffs are just and reasonable or file reforms addressing gigawatt-scale requests, co-located generation and cost allocation. Parties wishing to intervene in the proceedings faced a July 9 deadline, with formal RTO/ISO responses due August 17 and generation adequacy reports due July 20.

Why it matters: This is the federal government's most aggressive move yet to standardize how AI data centres connect to the grid nationwide, and its outcome will shape interconnection speed and cost allocation for every hyperscale project in the country.

2

Virginia's first-in-the-nation data centre electricity tax takes effect

EnergyPolicy

A new Virginia law taking effect July 1 imposes a 1.1-cent-per-kilowatt-hour consumption tax on all electricity used by data centres, including self-generated and behind-the-meter power, collected monthly by utilities and remitted to the State Corporation Commission. Revenue is capped at $600 million a year, with any excess refunded to the data centres, and the tax sunsets June 30, 2028 absent renewal.

Why it matters: As the country's largest data centre market, Virginia taxing power consumption directly — rather than just exempting equipment sales tax as before — creates a template other power-strained states are likely to copy.

Aecon-led consortium wins multibillion-dollar contract to build Alberta gas plant powering Meta's campus

CanadaEnergyConstruction

Aecon Group announced July 2 that its TRA consortium with Técnicas Reunidas Alberta was awarded the EPC contract for the Greenlight Electricity Centre, a $3.2 billion gas-fired combined-cycle plant in Sturgeon County that will initially generate 932MW, expandable to a permitted 1,864MW, to supply Meta's under-construction data centre.

Why it matters: The contract award turns Meta's headline-grabbing Alberta announcement into a concrete, multibillion-dollar Canadian construction and gas-demand story, cementing Alberta's role as a continental hub for both AI compute and natural gas power generation.

4

Aalo Atomics' microreactor reaches criticality, purpose-built for AI data centre power

TechEnergy

Aalo Atomics' Critical Test Reactor achieved initial criticality at Idaho National Laboratory at 12:20am on July 4, becoming the fourth US microreactor to beat a White House deadline requiring at least three advanced reactors to reach criticality by Independence Day — built in under eight months from groundbreaking. Aalo designs its reactors specifically for AI data centres, packaging 10MWe units into modular 50MWe "Aalo Pods," with a second commercial-scale unit under Project Ascension slated to power an on-site data centre by 2027.

Why it matters: A commercial-scale microreactor going from groundbreaking to fission in under eight months shows advanced nuclear is starting to move at a timeline that could actually match AI data centres' power needs, not just their long-term ambitions.

Kodiak Gas Services and Baker Hughes ink deal for up to 1.8GW of turbines for US data centres

Energy

Kodiak Gas Services and Baker Hughes announced a multi-year agreement July 8 covering an initial roughly 1 gigawatt of behind-the-meter power generation equipment — including NovaLT16 and Frame 5 gas turbines with BRUSH generators — to be delivered by 2030, with a broader framework providing a pathway to as much as 1.8GW over time.

Why it matters: Locking in multi-year turbine supply at gigawatt scale shows equipment manufacturers racing to secure capacity ahead of demand, as behind-the-meter gas generation becomes the default bridge fuel for data centres unable to wait on grid interconnection queues.

Local data centre opposition groups surge to 430, up from 76 at the end of 2025

Policy

A tracker from the Coalition for Responsible Data Center Development, Evitable and Together Against AI found 430 local opposition groups now active across more than 40 states, with combined membership topping 525,000 — nearly a sevenfold increase since December. Data Center Watch separately found opponents blocked or delayed at least 75 projects worth roughly $130 billion in the first quarter of 2026 alone, the highest quarterly total since it began tracking in 2023.

Why it matters: Community opposition has scaled from a handful of local disputes to a nationwide movement capable of blocking tens of billions of dollars in projects, making site selection and community engagement a first-order risk for every hyperscale developer.

Finance

Bond issuance backing AI investment has topped $250 billion in 2026, including $31.9 billion in high-yield bonds through July 8 — nearly all of it funding new data centre construction — plus roughly $218 billion in investment-grade debt from hyperscalers, chipmakers and data centre developers. Why it matters: With recent high-yield offerings from SpaceX and Meta trading below par after pricing, the sheer scale of debt now funding the AI buildout is starting to test investor appetite, a key signal for whether financing stays cheap enough to sustain the pace of construction.

Nashville Metro Council advances data centre zoning crackdown after marathon public hearing

Policy

More than 100 Nashvillians testified against data centre development at a July 7 Metro Council hearing that ran past midnight, with 245 residents signed up to speak under a ticketed queuing system. Why it matters: A major Southern metro moving toward an outright size cap and buffer-zone regime, not just a temporary pause, would mark one of the most restrictive permanent data centre zoning regimes adopted by a large US city to date.

Policy

Why it matters: A county acting pre-emptively against a single inquiry, before any formal application, shows local governments increasingly treating data centres as a threat to be blocked outright rather than a project to be negotiated.
