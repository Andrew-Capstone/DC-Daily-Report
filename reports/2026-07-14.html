Meta to begin production of in-house "Iris" AI chip in September

Tech

An internal memo reviewed by Reuters shows Meta will start manufacturing its custom "Iris" AI accelerator in September, the first of four planned MTIA chip generations designed with Broadcom and fabricated by TSMC, which cleared bug-testing in about six weeks. The memo outlines a two-step capacity expansion — 7GW of compute online in 2026, doubling to 14GW by 2027 — as Meta's AI infrastructure spend climbs toward $145 billion this year.

Why it matters: A hyperscaler successfully fielding its own silicon on a roughly six-month cadence, rather than a yearly one, would loosen the industry's dependence on Nvidia and AMD and reshape how fast AI compute capacity can scale.

Meta expands Louisiana's Hyperion campus to 5GW, ups investment to $50 billion

EnergyFinance

Meta announced July 13 it is expanding its Hyperion data centre campus in Richland Parish, Louisiana from 2GW to 5GW of capacity, more than doubling its planned investment to as much as $50 billion, plus $1 billion for local roads and water systems. The buildout is paired with Entergy funding for seven new natural gas plants, three grid-scale batteries and nuclear upgrades, which Meta says will save local ratepayers $2 billion over two decades.

Why it matters: It's one of the largest single AI campus commitments on record, and the tied-in utility investment is a template hyperscalers are increasingly using to blunt local backlash over grid costs.

Google signs vPPA for full 2.45GW output of Arkansas solar-plus-storage project

Energy

Google agreed to a virtual power purchase agreement covering the entire output of Cypress Creek Renewables' Steel River Energy Center in Mississippi County, Arkansas, a $3.5 billion project that will pair 2.45GW of solar with 2.9GWh of battery storage, with a first 1.6GW phase targeted for 2029. Google said the deal, its second in the state after a 100MW agreement with Treaty Oak, supplies the broader grid rather than a dedicated facility.

Why it matters: Locking in an entire solar-plus-storage project's output gives Cypress Creek the revenue certainty to finance construction, a financing pattern increasingly required to get net-new generation built fast enough for AI demand.

Infineon and LS Electric team up on DC power infrastructure for AI data centres

TechEnergy

Infineon and South Korea's LS Electric signed a non-binding MOU to jointly develop direct-current power infrastructure for AI facilities, focused on power conversion for energy storage, solid-state transformers and solid-state circuit breakers, combining Infineon's semiconductor expertise with LS Electric's systems integration and its 60% share of South Korea's commercial data centre power market.

Why it matters: High-efficiency DC architectures cut conversion losses compared to legacy AC designs, and a semiconductor-and-systems pairing like this could push DC power distribution from niche pilots toward a mainstream option for next-generation AI facilities.

Canada's data centre pipeline could reach 20GW, government document shows

Canada

A federal briefing document prepared for international investment outreach puts Canada's proposed and in-development data centre capacity at more than 20GW, BNN Bloomberg reported July 8. Officials cautioned the figure reflects early-stage private-sector proposals rather than a construction forecast, since many projects still face financing, regulatory, electricity-availability and Indigenous and municipal consultation hurdles.

Why it matters: The figure gives the clearest public signal yet of how aggressively Canada is being pitched to global AI infrastructure investors, even as individual projects run into the same local resistance seen in the US.

Data centres have already added $23 billion to electricity bills in PJM territory

EnergyPolicy

A PJM Monitoring Analytics report finds data centre demand has driven $23 billion in customer price increases across the 14-state PJM market, with elevated costs expected to persist through at least 2028, Fortune reported July 14. The piece notes data centres can curtail consumption during peak demand to reduce their own cost exposure, potentially shifting a larger share of grid costs onto residential ratepayers who lack dedicated representation in utility rate cases.

Why it matters: Hard numbers on ratepayer impact give ammunition to the moratorium and cost-allocation fights spreading across state legislatures, and explain why the White House and states like Virginia are moving on the issue simultaneously.

10

Virginia's first-in-nation data centre electricity tax takes effect

PolicyEnergy

Virginia's new tax on data centre electricity consumption — 1.1 cents per kilowatt-hour, capped at $600 million a year and sunsetting in mid-2028 — took effect July 1, with first payments due in September, Forbes reported July 13. The levy, which covers utility, retail and self-generated power including behind-the-meter sources, was driven by fears among lawmakers in the country's largest data centre market that ratepayers would otherwise absorb rising grid costs.

Why it matters: As the first US state to tax data centre power use directly, Virginia's approach is likely to become the model — or cautionary tale — other data-centre-heavy states study as they weigh their own ratepayer-protection measures.

Finance

A Bloomberg Opinion analysis published July 14 cites Goldman Sachs estimates that Alphabet, Amazon, Meta, Microsoft and Oracle will collectively spend $5.8 trillion on AI computing capacity through 2030, and argues the debt financing racing to fund that build-out is inconsistent — some deals include strong protections against construction delays or troubled tenants, while others offer investors far less.

Why it matters: As data centre debt issuance keeps setting records, uneven underwriting standards raise the risk that a slowdown in AI demand or a single high-profile default could hit bondholders harder than current pricing implies.
