1

Meta breaks ground on its first Canadian data centre, a $13B CAD campus in Alberta

CanadaEnergyConstruction

Meta announced July 8 it is building a new 1GW, AI-optimized data centre in Sturgeon County, Alberta — its first data centre in Canada and 33rd globally — representing an investment of more than CAD $13 billion. The project will employ roughly 3,000 workers at peak construction and 300+ permanently, use a closed-loop cooling system with no ongoing water draw, and includes about CAD $60 million for local roads, water systems and community grants.

Why it matters: This is Meta's largest data centre outside the US and a landmark moment for Canada's AI infrastructure ambitions, cementing Alberta's position as the country's hyperscale hub.

2

Meta's custom "Iris" AI chip enters production in September, targeting 14GW of compute

Tech

An internal Meta memo reported July 9 shows the company's in-house data centre accelerator, code-named Iris and part of its four-generation MTIA program, will begin manufacturing in September after six weeks of testing turned up no major issues. Meta is co-designing the chip with Broadcom and manufacturing it via TSMC as part of a plan to roughly double computing capacity to 14GW next year.

Why it matters: A working custom silicon program at this scale would meaningfully cut Meta's dependence on Nvidia and AMD and lower the cost per unit of AI compute across its entire data centre fleet.

Capital Power signs 250MW long-term power deal to supply Meta's Alberta campus

CanadaEnergy

Capital Power announced July 8 — the same day as Meta's groundbreaking — a long-term Energy Supply Agreement to provide 250 megawatts of capacity and energy in support of Meta's new Sturgeon County data centre, adding to the Greenlight Electricity Centre gas plant already under construction to serve the site.

Why it matters: Stacking multiple dedicated power agreements onto a single campus shows how hyperscalers are diversifying supply rather than relying on one generator, a pattern likely to repeat as more gigawatt-scale Canadian projects move forward.

GridMarket and Deployable Energy strike $22.5B nuclear pipeline for data centres through 2035

EnergyTech

GridMarket and microreactor maker Deployable Energy announced a partnership July 7 to commercialize Deployable's Unity Nuclear Battery — which achieved criticality the prior week — across GridMarket's hyperscaler and industrial customer base, targeting 500MW of new nuclear capacity deployed annually from 2030 through 2035, an estimated $145 billion in lifetime contract value.

Why it matters: Pairing a newly critical microreactor design with an existing project-facilitation platform could turn nuclear power for data centres from one-off pilot deals into a repeatable, at-scale product.

5

Valar Atomics and Nvidia explore nearly waterless, SMR-powered AI data centre in Utah

TechEnergy

Valar Atomics and Nvidia announced July 1 they are collaborating on a proposed 30-megawatt data centre in Utah powered by Valar's Ward250 small modular reactor and cooled using Nvidia's water-efficient technology, aiming for a facility that draws almost no water while running on dedicated nuclear power.

Why it matters: Combining a small reactor with next-generation low-water cooling directly targets the two resources — power and water — that most often trigger community opposition to new data centres.

6

Texas data centres use dry-cleaner-style permits to dodge review on power plants that could rival 35 coal plants

PolicyEnergy

A Texas Tribune investigation published July 9 found at least 38 Texas data centres have obtained "minor" or "permit by rule" air permits — a category designed for small operations like dry cleaners — to authorize more than 2,100 backup generators statewide while avoiding full environmental review. Nine planned data centre power plants alone are permitted to emit over 130 million tons of greenhouse gases a year, more than most countries and equivalent to roughly 35 coal-fired plants; OpenAI's Abilene Stargate site could grow to 51 turbines and generators.

Why it matters: A "small first, big later" permitting pattern that sidesteps public input on facilities emitting at national-scale levels is likely to become a flashpoint for federal and state regulators well beyond Texas.
Texas Tribune →
7

Leaked federal document puts Canada's data centre pipeline at 20GW; Ottawa pushes back

CanadaPolicy

A federal presentation obtained by The Canadian Press and reported July 8 shows Canada has roughly 337MW of AI data centre capacity operating today against more than 20GW of projects proposed or in planning, most concentrated in Alberta. Industry Minister Evan Solomon's office disputed the figure's significance, saying it was never used externally and that the national AI strategy projects a far more modest 5.5GW of commercial demand by 2030.

Why it matters: The gap between the headline pipeline number and the government's own demand forecast underscores how speculative much of the announced Canadian buildout still is, even as Alberta races ahead on approvals.

TeraWulf plans $3.5B debt raise for its Anthropic-leased Kentucky data centre

Finance

Bloomberg reported July 9 that former bitcoin miner TeraWulf is lining up about $3.5 billion in leveraged loans and high-yield bonds, led by Morgan Stanley, to build out its Justified Data campus in Hawesville, Kentucky, where subsidiary Raylan Data signed a 20-year lease with Anthropic on July 6 expected to generate roughly $19 billion in contracted revenue.

Why it matters: It is TeraWulf's first entry into the leveraged loan market and another example of AI labs' data centre leases becoming the collateral underpinning a fast-growing new category of infrastructure debt.

National Grid Ventures invests $1.75B in Joulent to accelerate AI data centre power

EnergyFinance

National Grid Ventures agreed July 1 to invest $1.75 billion for a 35% stake in Joulent, whose flagship project is the 2.67GW Project Kilby facility being co-developed with Chevron to power a Microsoft data centre campus near Pecos, Texas under a 20-year agreement beginning in 2028. Joulent's platform bundles co-located gas generation, batteries and renewables to speed up power delivery outside the traditional utility interconnection queue.

Why it matters: A major transmission utility taking an equity stake in a behind-the-meter power developer shows incumbent grid players betting directly on speed-to-power, not just interconnection, as the AI infrastructure bottleneck.

10

Texas regulators' "Batch Zero" process for connecting gigawatt-scale data centres takes effect

EnergyPolicy

The Public Utility Commission of Texas's approval of ERCOT's new "Batch Zero" large-load connection process took effect July 11, grouping qualified projects of 75MW or larger into single studies so ERCOT can assess demand and plan transmission upgrades collectively rather than case by case. ERCOT is the first US grid operator to use a batch approach; it is currently tracking over 438,000MW of large-load requests, nearly 90% of it from data centres, with applicant classifications due by August.

Why it matters: As the busiest data centre interconnection queue in the country, how Texas manages this batch process will likely become the template other power-constrained grid operators look to next.
ERCOT.com →

Finance

12

Microsoft and Chevron's Project Kilby: a 2.67GW gas-powered campus in West Texas

EnergyConstruction

Chevron subsidiary Energy Forge One and investment firm Engine No. 1 signed a 20-year power purchase agreement with Microsoft on June 22 to build Project Kilby, a co-located natural gas plant near Pecos, Texas that will phase in up to 2.67GW to supply a new Microsoft data centre campus. Microsoft called it one of the largest single capacity additions in its history, expecting over 6,000 construction jobs at peak, with power beginning to flow in late 2028.

Why it matters: The scale — nearly 2.7GW behind a single dedicated gas plant — illustrates how far hyperscalers are now willing to go to build their own power supply rather than wait on grid interconnection.

13

Wisconsin regulators refuse to loosen data centre credit rules, setting up Oracle court fight

PolicyEnergy

Wisconsin's Public Service Commission declined July 10 to reconsider credit-rating requirements that force data centre developers with ratings below A- to post cash or credit guarantees before receiving electric service, rejecting a loosening request from utility We Energies. Oracle, rated BBB, faces more than $100 million a year in required guarantees for its Port Washington project and is challenging the rule in Ozaukee County Circuit Court, arguing the commission overstepped its authority.

Why it matters: The outcome will test whether states can force data centre developers — not ratepayers — to bear the financial risk of the bespoke power plants their projects increasingly require.

15

Nearly a third of Indiana counties have moved to restrict data centres

Policy

A July 6 analysis by Indiana University's Environmental Resilience Institute found 11 Indiana counties now have data centre ordinances, at least 17 have imposed temporary moratoriums, and two — Marshall and Cass counties — have banned new data centres outright, roughly a third of the state's 92 counties taking some restrictive action so far in 2026.

Why it matters: Indiana has actively courted hyperscale investment, so a state-level tally showing a third of counties pumping the brakes shows the moratorium wave has moved well past traditional hotspot states into the industry's own target markets.

Fort Worth zoning commission rejects first-of-its-kind data centre ordinance as too weak

Policy

Fort Worth's Zoning Commission voted 7-4 on July 8 to deny a draft ordinance that would have banned cryptocurrency mining, restricted data centres to industrial zones, and required 250-foot setbacks from homes, sending it back for stricter standards ahead of a revised version due August 4 and a City Council vote August 11.

Why it matters: Commissioners rejecting their own staff-drafted rules as too permissive — rather than too restrictive — suggests Texas's largest cities are trending toward tighter, not looser, data centre zoning even as the state courts the industry at the grid level.

18

Warren, Maine voters nearly unanimously approve 180-day data centre moratorium

Policy

Residents of Warren, Maine approved an 180-day data centre moratorium at a July 8 special town meeting that drew an overflow crowd of about 60 people, nearly double June's annual meeting turnout, citing concerns over water use and heated wastewater discharge into local rivers. The pause gives the town time to draft ordinances before any application arrives and can be extended in further 180-day increments.

Why it matters: A small New England town acting preemptively, with no project even proposed, illustrates how the moratorium playbook has become a default first move well beyond the traditional Sun Belt and Mid-Atlantic hotspots.

Sanders and Ocasio-Cortez's federal AI Data Center Moratorium Act reaches both chambers

Policy

Rep. Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez introduced a House companion on June 24 to Sen. Bernie Sanders's Senate bill, which would halt construction or expansion of AI data centres over 20MW until federal safeguards are in place covering utility costs, climate impact, community approval rights and union labor, with quarterly Energy Department reporting on water and power use. The bill is co-sponsored by Reps. André Carson, Rashida Tlaib and several other progressives.

Why it matters: Though unlikely to pass this Congress, a national moratorium bill reaching both chambers shows the local opposition movement now has an organized federal legislative vehicle, raising the odds of similar measures resurfacing.

20

Michigan township planners recommend moratorium power after hundreds pack data centre hearing

Policy

Garfield Township's Planning Commission in Grand Traverse County, Michigan voted July 8 to recommend a zoning change giving the township board authority to freeze data centre applications, permits and rezonings for up to 12 months, after nearly 40 residents spoke at a packed hearing — none in opposition to the moratorium power itself. No data centre is currently proposed for the township.

Why it matters: Local governments increasingly want moratorium authority pre-approved and ready to use rather than drafted reactively once a project is announced, compressing the window developers have to build community support.
