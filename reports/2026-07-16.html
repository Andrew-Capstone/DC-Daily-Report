1

Meta to put in-house "Iris" AI chip into production in September, targeting 14GW of compute

TechFinance

Meta plans to begin manufacturing its custom AI chip, code-named Iris, in September as part of the Meta Training and Inference Accelerator (MTIA) family designed in-house with Broadcom and built by TSMC, Reuters and CNBC reported July 9. The chip is meant to supplement, not replace, Nvidia and AMD GPUs as Meta aims to double its computing capacity from 7GW in 2026 to 14GW in 2027, backed by a raised 2026 capex forecast of up to $145 billion.

Why it matters: A hyperscaler moving to a roughly six-month, four-generation custom-silicon cadence signals AI infrastructure economics are shifting away from near-total Nvidia dependency, a trend that will reshape chip demand and margins across the industry.

2

Meta's Louisiana "Hyperion" data centre expands to 5GW as investment tops $50 billion

EnergyFinance

Meta confirmed its Hyperion supercluster in Richland Parish, Louisiana will scale to 5GW of capacity with total investment exceeding $50 billion, up sharply from the $27 billion disclosed last October and the original $10 billion estimate, CNBC reported July 13. Blue Owl Capital holds an 80% stake in the joint venture, and the project — backed by a 20-year state sales-tax exemption — has already awarded more than $1.6 billion in contracts to local businesses.

Why it matters: A fivefold cost escalation on a single campus illustrates how fast AI infrastructure budgets are growing even for the best-capitalized hyperscalers, intensifying scrutiny of the tax breaks and power commitments underwriting these megaprojects.

Data centre operator Switch lines up potential $80 billion IPO

Finance

Switch has hired Goldman Sachs and JPMorgan Chase to lead a potential IPO that could raise as much as $10 billion as soon as the fourth quarter, valuing the company at nearly $80 billion including debt, Reuters reported July 13-14. The operator — taken private by DigitalBridge and IFM Investors for $11 billion in 2022 and powered by renewable energy since 2016 — counts Nvidia, Dell and FedEx among its data-centre clients, though size, timing and valuation remain under discussion.

Why it matters: A potential top-tier US listing at nearly $80 billion would rank among the largest stock debuts in years, a fresh signal that public markets remain eager to underwrite AI infrastructure even as private capital already floods the sector.

4

Alberta's AESO opens 1.6GW temporary power pool for gas-fired data centres

CanadaEnergy

The Alberta Electric System Operator is proposing a new 1.6-gigawatt "bring-your-own-generation" bridging pool that would let data centre developers tap temporary grid power ahead of building their own plants — but only if they commit to natural gas, The Logic reported. Access would scale with milestones like permitting and construction starts, the power would be first curtailed in a supply shortfall, and AESO is targeting an August rollout after immediate consultations.

Why it matters: By restricting bridging power to gas-fired projects, Alberta is effectively picking a fuel-source winner for its AI data-centre boom, a policy choice other provinces and states weighing their own interim-power rules will be watching closely.

5

Hamilton council votes down what would have been Canada's first data centre moratorium

CanadaPolicy

Hamilton city council rejected a proposed one-year interim control bylaw pausing new AI data centres by a 10-6 vote July 15, reversing the direction it set on June 24 when it voted 15-1 to draft the pause, CP24 and CBC reported. The reversal followed closed-door legal advice that a McMaster University-linked project would likely have been exempt anyway, prompting Mayor Andrea Horwath to warn the pause would "hold back Hamilton," while sponsor Councillor Nrinder Nann said it was meant to buy time to upgrade zoning tools.

Why it matters: Hamilton's about-face removes the template other Canadian municipalities were watching for a legally durable pause, leaving Canada without a passed data-centre moratorium even as pressure builds federally and in Quebec and B.C.

6

Keel Infrastructure advances 96MW Sherbrooke, Québec data centre project

CanadaEnergy

Keel Infrastructure received Sherbrooke municipal approval and an agreement with Hydro-Sherbrooke to consolidate three existing Bitcoin-mining sites into a single 96MW campus, reclassifying the power from cryptocurrency mining to high-performance computing and AI use without requesting additional capacity, according to a July 15 report. The company also signed a purchase agreement for land roughly 100 miles east of Montreal, with the deal's closing expected in the first quarter of 2027 pending site and municipal approvals.

Why it matters: Converting already-powered crypto-mining sites into AI data centres sidesteps the grid-interconnection queues slowing new projects across Canada, offering a faster and less contentious path to HPC capacity.

7

DOE approves $3.26 billion loan for Texas grid overhaul as Abbott presses data centres to fund their own power

EnergyPolicy

The US Department of Energy finalized a $3.26 billion loan to AEP Texas to rebuild and expand roughly 2,800 miles of transmission lines across about 100 projects, aiming to double capacity for growing demand from data centres and Permian Basin oil and gas operations, Fox 7 Austin reported July 14. Energy Secretary Chris Wright said the investment will "support the energy needed for AI, advanced manufacturing, the Permian Basin," and it follows Governor Greg Abbott's recent push for stricter data-centre rules, including requirements that facilities fund their own interconnection and water systems.

Why it matters: Federal grid financing paired with a governor-driven cost-shift onto developers shows even the most data-centre-friendly states are moving to insulate ratepayers as the AI buildout collides with grid capacity limits.

8

Palm Beach County rejects "Project Tango" AI data centre after 12-hour hearing

Policy

Palm Beach County commissioners voted 4-1 to deny the 3.6-million-square-foot "Project Tango" hyperscale data centre near Arden after a marathon overnight hearing that drew overflow crowds and more than 80 public speakers, WFLX reported July 16. The denial is "without prejudice," so developer PBA Holdings can revise and resubmit, and the property retains prior 2016 approvals for other large-scale development.

Why it matters: A near-unanimous denial after such an extended fight shows even fast-growing Sun Belt counties are hitting a political ceiling on hyperscale projects sited near homes and schools, regardless of prior industrial entitlements.

9

Pulaski County, Arkansas advances weaker moratorium carving out AVAIO Digital's $6 billion project

PolicyFinance

The Pulaski County Quorum Court rejected a stricter data-centre moratorium 6-3 but then passed an alternative version 8-1 that explicitly exempts AVAIO Digital's planned $6 billion, 150-megawatt Wrightsville campus, the Arkansas Times reported July 14. Sponsor Dianne Curry said the pause is meant "solely to preserve the status quo while regulations are evaluated," while the county's deputy attorney warned neither version shields the county from future litigation.

Why it matters: Carving the largest active project out of its own moratorium shows how thoroughly a single developer's leverage can reshape "pause" legislation meant to apply to future projects broadly.

10

$5.1 billion "Oakline" data centre proposal for Salem, Oregon draws praise and pushback

Construction

California-based Verrus publicly unveiled plans for Oakline at Mill Creek, a three-building, $5.1 billion data centre campus in southeast Salem, following a 16-month confidentiality agreement with the city, KATU reported. Construction unions and city estimates pointing to roughly $10.5 million in combined annual city revenue welcomed the project, while opponents criticized the limited public notice and raised water and environmental concerns; Verrus says the design uses battery storage and closed-loop cooling to cut water and peak-grid demand.

Why it matters: A 16-month confidential negotiation preceding public disclosure is becoming a flashpoint in its own right, adding transparency to water and power as a core grievance driving local opposition nationwide.

11

Rural Hall, North Carolina residents rally against "Project Iron Spur" ahead of July 30 vote

PolicyConstruction

Dozens of residents gathered at Rural Hall Moravian Church on July 14 to oppose a proposed 130-acre, three-building data centre and substation known as "Project Iron Spur," even after the town council reversed course in June and passed a resolution supporting the project in exchange for annexation and tax revenue, ABC45 reported. The Forsyth County Planning Board voted 7-2 in June to recommend denial of the rezoning, but the County Board of Commissioners holds the final vote on July 30.

Why it matters: A town reversing its own opposition once annexation revenue is on the table, only to face renewed resident backlash, illustrates the tension between municipal budgets and community concerns that is playing out in small towns nationwide.

12

Putnam County, Florida adopts data-centre disclosure policy instead of a moratorium

Policy

Putnam County commissioners unanimously approved a policy requiring staff to report any data-centre development inquiries to the county administrator, who must notify all commissioners and post the information online within 72 hours, the Palatka Daily News reported July 14. Commissioner Josh Alexander, who proposed the measure, said it is meant to let "constituents know who's courting our community," while the county attorney continues researching whether a full moratorium would expose the county to legal risk.

Why it matters: A lighter-touch disclosure rule gives counties wary of moratorium litigation a faster, lower-risk way to get ahead of secretive site-scouting, and could become a template for jurisdictions not ready for an outright pause.

13

Botetourt County, Virginia water-planning fight over data centre tabled twice amid protests

Policy

About 50 residents packed a Botetourt County planning commission session on July 13 to demand transparency on water usage tied to a proposed data centre, with protesters both inside and outside the building, WDBJ7 reported. The discussion was tabled twice — first from the work session to the evening hearing, then again to the commission's next meeting — after county leadership had earlier declined the planning commission's request for a dedicated water-transparency meeting.

Why it matters: Repeated postponements on a basic water-usage disclosure request show how procedural delay itself is becoming a point of friction between residents and officials in data-centre fights, independent of the underlying project's merits.

14

Massillon, Ohio tightens proposed data centre zoning rules, delays final vote

Policy

Massillon City Council delayed final action on its data-centre zoning ordinance by at least two weeks after adding amendments that would restrict all data centres to heavy-industrial zoning, push residential setbacks to at least 400 feet (up from 200), and require developers to prove adequate water, electric and wastewater capacity before construction. The revised ordinance is expected to return through the Planning and Zoning Commission before coming back to council, likely in early August.

Why it matters: Rather than a blanket pause, Massillon's approach of hardening zoning and utility-capacity requirements offers a middle path between outright moratoriums and unrestricted approvals that other mid-sized cities may look to replicate.
