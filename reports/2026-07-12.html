1

SK Hynix raises $26.5B in the biggest foreign IPO in US history to fund AI memory production

TechFinance

SK Hynix sold 177.9 million American Depositary Shares at $149 each on July 10, raising $26.5 billion in the largest-ever US debut by a non-American company, topping Alibaba's 2014 IPO. The chipmaker, a primary high-bandwidth-memory supplier to Nvidia, will use proceeds for a new South Korean fab, a packaging facility and EUV scanners to address AI-driven memory shortages, while US officials are separately pressing SK Hynix to build new American fabs.

Why it matters: The record-breaking raise shows public capital markets are now directly financing the memory-chip bottleneck underpinning every hyperscale AI data centre buildout, not just private and project debt.

Micron lifts total US chip investment to $250B, pours first concrete on record New York fab

TechFinanceConstruction

Micron announced July 9-10 it is expanding its domestic investment commitment to roughly $250 billion through 2035 — about $50 billion more than previously planned — and unveiled a $500 million partnership plus 10-year supply agreement with Taiwan's GlobalWafers to expand silicon wafer manufacturing in Texas. The company also confirmed it has poured first concrete at its Clay, New York fabrication facility, which it calls the largest semiconductor manufacturing campus in US history; shares rose nearly 7% on the news.

Why it matters: Securing the upstream silicon-wafer supply chain, not just GPU capacity, is becoming the next constraint hyperscalers and memory makers are racing to lock down as AI data centre demand accelerates.

Pembina-led consortium sanctions $4.6B Greenlight gas plant to power Meta's Alberta campus

CanadaEnergy

Pembina Pipeline confirmed July 8 that it has reached a positive final investment decision, alongside Morgan Stanley Infrastructure Partners and Kineticor Asset Management, on the Greenlight Electricity Centre — a dedicated, behind-the-meter gas-to-power plant that will supply Meta's new Sturgeon County data centre under a long-term tolling agreement. Meta will not own the plant itself; the consortium separately confirmed the campus will draw roughly 150 million cubic feet of natural gas per day at full 1.8GW build-out.

Why it matters: This is the primary power source behind Meta's landmark Canadian campus, and it shows pipeline and infrastructure-fund capital, not just utilities, increasingly building bespoke gas plants to win hyperscale tolling contracts.

4

North Carolina repeals data centre electricity tax exemption, keeps capital incentives

PolicyEnergy

Governor Josh Stein signed North Carolina's 2026 budget this week, ending the state's sales-and-use tax exemption on electricity purchased by data centres while preserving exemptions on qualifying equipment and other capital investment. The change is projected to generate $21.4 million in additional revenue in fiscal 2026-27, rising to $28.6 million by 2030-31.

Why it matters: North Carolina joins a growing list of states drawing a line between rewarding construction and subsidizing open-ended electricity consumption, a distinction likely to spread as more states rewrite their incentive playbooks.

5

Virginia's first-in-the-nation data centre electricity tax takes effect

PolicyEnergy

Virginia's new consumption tax of $0.011 per kilowatt-hour on electricity used by data centres took effect July 1, becoming the nation's first tax of its kind. The tax, collected through monthly utility invoices and capped with refunds if collections exceed $600 million a year, would cost a continuously operating 500MW facility roughly $48 million annually.

Why it matters: As the country's largest data centre market, Virginia's move to directly tax power consumption — rather than just capital investment — sets a template other power-constrained states are already watching.

DeepInfra opens its first data centre outside the US in Toronto

CanadaTech

AI inference cloud provider DeepInfra announced July 9 it has deployed a 1.7MW facility packed with more than 1,000 Nvidia Blackwell B300 GPUs in Toronto — its ninth data centre location overall and first outside the United States, following a $107 million Series B round. CEO Nikola Borisov said the expansion reflects enterprises moving from AI experimentation to production at a pace that demands globally distributed infrastructure.

Why it matters: A dedicated inference-cloud operator picking Toronto for its first international site is another sign Canada is becoming a real node in the AI compute map, not just a hyperscaler power play.

7

Hamilton council votes 15-1 to advance what would be Canada's first city-wide data centre moratorium

CanadaPolicy

Hamilton city council voted 15-1 to advance a proposed year-long, renewable moratorium on new data centres, rejecting a 14-2 bid to exempt smaller research-focused facilities tied to a planned project at the former Hamilton Spectator building. A final council vote is scheduled for next month; the pause follows a committee's rejection of Slate Asset Management's plan to carve out part of its harbourfront "Steelport" redevelopment for a data centre campus.

Why it matters: If it passes, Hamilton would become the first Canadian city to adopt a data centre moratorium, a milestone that could embolden other Canadian municipalities watching the US wave of local pauses.

Big Tech doubles its debt load to $350B funding the AI data centre spending spree

Finance

Bloomberg reported July 10 that Alphabet, Amazon, Meta, Microsoft and Oracle have collectively added roughly $350 billion in debt over the past five years, doubling their combined obligations as they race to fund gigawatt-scale AI infrastructure. The US high-yield bond market alone has absorbed $31.9 billion in new AI-related bonds through July 8 this year, nearly all of it backing new data centres.

Why it matters: The scale of leverage now underpinning the AI buildout is drawing comparisons to prior infrastructure debt cycles and raising questions about how exposed credit markets have become to a single sector's growth bet.

10

Study: data centre opponents have blocked or delayed $130B in projects so far in 2026

Policy

A Data Center Watch study recirculated widely July 10 found opponents blocked or delayed at least 75 US data centre projects worth about $130 billion in the first quarter of 2026 alone — roughly matching all of 2025 — with active opposition groups more than doubling to 833 across 49 states. Researchers called it a structural shift rather than a cyclical spike, driven by an increasingly organized opposition playbook and over 300 statehouse bills introduced in the year's first six weeks.

Why it matters: With opposition now blocking capital at a pace matching entire prior years, community pushback has become a first-order risk factor for developers, not a fringe obstacle.

A new data centre outside Lufkin leaves another East Texas community bracing for a boom

PolicyConstruction

Land outside Lufkin, Texas has been sold to Denver-based AmpZ Champion Data Center Holdings and Atlanta-based EPG Champion Development for a planned data centre that could bring roughly $1 billion in private investment and 500 construction jobs, the Tyler Morning Telegraph reported July 12. Why it matters: Rural East Texas has become one of the most active — and contested — data centre corridors in the country, and this project shows the pattern of rapid land deals outpacing public engagement continuing to repeat.

Tech

15

Nvidia's Rubin generation runs "hotter than a hot tub" to cut AI data centre water use to near zero

TechEnergy

Nvidia detailed its Rubin-generation data centre design, the first to be 100% liquid-cooled with no fans anywhere in the system, running coolant at 45°C so outdoor dry coolers can reject heat without water-hungry chillers for most of the year. Nvidia estimates a 50MW hyperscale facility could save over $4 million a year in cooling-related energy and water costs by adopting the design.

Why it matters: Eliminating water chillers at the reference-design level for Nvidia's next GPU generation could reset the baseline for how much water and power new AI campuses need industry-wide.
