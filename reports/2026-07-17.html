1

TSMC pledges additional $100 billion for Arizona, pushing total US commitment to $265 billion

TechFinance

TSMC announced at its July 16 Q2 earnings conference in Taipei that it will invest an additional $100 billion in Arizona for at least four more 2-nanometer-and-below fabs plus advanced packaging plants, bringing its total pledged US investment to $265 billion across as many as 10 fabs. The chipmaker posted a record quarterly profit on surging AI demand and raised its full-year 2026 capex budget to $60-64 billion from a prior $52-56 billion.

Why it matters: A $100 billion top-up for Arizona chip capacity shows the AI data-centre boom is now reshaping where the world's most advanced silicon gets made, not just where it gets deployed.

2

ACS and BlackRock's GIP launch Coravel data centre platform, sign first hyperscaler deal

FinanceConstruction

Spanish construction group ACS and Global Infrastructure Partners formed a €2 billion ($2.33 billion) 50:50 joint venture called Coravel, combining power strategy, site development, construction, capital and operations under one organization with a 1.7GW development portfolio across the US, Europe and Australia, DCD reported. Coravel immediately signed its first hyperscaler customer for roughly 140MW of critical IT load across three facilities at a Dallas-Fort Worth campus, with an option to expand by another 100MW.

Why it matters: A construction giant and BlackRock's infrastructure arm combining site development, capital and operations into one platform — and landing a hyperscaler customer on day one — signals data-centre delivery is consolidating into fewer, better-capitalized players.

3

Microsoft's $3.3 billion Wisconsin "Fairwater" AI campus goes fully operational

TechConstruction

Microsoft's first completed data centre at its Mount Pleasant, Wisconsin campus — built on the site of the failed Foxconn manufacturing project — is now fully operational after a two-year build involving nearly 10,000 construction workers, Data Center Knowledge reported. Officials say the $3.3 billion "Fairwater" facility, which required 46.6 miles of deep foundation piles and 120 miles of underground cable, will house one of the world's most powerful AI supercomputers.

Why it matters: Bringing the flagship Fairwater campus fully online validates the multi-year, multibillion-dollar build model other hyperscalers are racing to replicate at speed.

4

Crusoe and Lancium announce 1.0-gigawatt AI data centre campus in Childress, Texas

ConstructionEnergy

Crusoe and Lancium unveiled plans for a 1GW AI data centre campus on 270 acres in Childress, Texas — their second partnership after Abilene, where the pair built OpenAI's Oracle-backed Stargate site, GlobeNewswire reported July 15. Under the model, Lancium owns the land and manages power interconnection and behind-the-meter solar and storage, while Crusoe designs, builds and operates the data centre; Lancium has floated up to 10 such sites in Childress at roughly $4 billion each.

Why it matters: A second Crusoe-Lancium campus, with plans for up to $40 billion in potential investment across the county, shows the co-located power-plus-compute model scaling well beyond its original Abilene template.

5

Elon Musk's Memphis AI empire becomes the epicenter of the data centre backlash

PolicyEnergy

Two years after Musk made Greater Memphis the hub of his AI ambitions, residents near the Colossus and Colossus II sites are grappling with noise and emissions from dozens of gas-burning turbines, CNBC reported July 16. The Southern Environmental Law Center and Earthjustice have sued xAI on behalf of the NAACP alleging Clean Air Act violations from unpermitted turbines, while Southaven, Mississippi residents separately filed a nuisance class action over noise "like a form of torture."

Why it matters: Two years after Musk made Memphis ground zero for speed-over-permitting AI buildout, the resulting lawsuits and noise complaints are becoming the cautionary case other communities cite when they fight new projects.

6

Vancouver council rejects motion requiring environmental review of AI data centres

CanadaPolicy

Vancouver city council voted 7-3 on July 16 to defeat a motion from Councillor Lucy Maloney that would have required third-party assessments of water, greenhouse gas, air, noise and heat impacts before rezoning or permitting new data centres, Canada's National Observer reported. More than twenty residents spoke in favour of the motion, citing a 26MW facility set to launch in Mount Pleasant by year-end and a 100MW facility planned next to BC Place, while the city remains under stage-3 water restrictions.

Why it matters: A rejected review requirement in a city already under water restrictions shows even AI-skeptical Canadian councils are struggling to translate public concern into binding rules.

7

Blackstone's QTS more than triples data-centre loan to $3.25 billion, scraps bond sale

Finance

QTS Realty Trust, the Blackstone-owned data-centre operator, more than tripled a proposed leveraged loan to $3.25 billion and canceled a planned $1 billion bond sale, Bloomberg reported July 15. JPMorgan is leading the loan, which will settle outstanding construction loans and cover corporate expenses, with QTS offering 2.25 percentage points over the benchmark rate versus roughly 94 basis points for comparably rated bonds.

Why it matters: Tripling a leveraged loan while scrapping a bond sale shows lenders' appetite for AI-infrastructure debt is outpacing even the bond market's, reshaping how data centres get financed.

8

St. Joseph County, Indiana rejects $13 billion New Carlisle data centre after 10-hour meeting

Policy

The St. Joseph County Council voted 7-2, after 4 a.m. following a marathon overnight session, to deny rezoning roughly 1,000 acres of farmland near New Carlisle, Indiana for a proposed $13 billion data centre, WSBT reported. The vote followed two prior "unfavorable" recommendations from the county's plan commission and a council committee; the developer has the option to resubmit in six months, and an Amazon data centre already partially operational sits nearby.

Why it matters: Killing a $13 billion project after a 10-hour, past-4-a.m. hearing shows how far grassroots opposition can go even in counties that already host major hyperscaler investment.

9

Boulder City, Nevada council votes to appeal BLM approval of data centre on federal land

Policy

Boulder City council voted unanimously July 14 to appeal the Bureau of Land Management's June 26 approval of the Townsite Data Center on 81 acres of BLM land in the Eldorado Valley, about three miles from residences, Nevada Current reported. The developer had originally received federal approval in 2023 for a solar-and-storage project on the site but won an amended right-of-way grant in 2025 to build a data centre instead; the city says it was never consulted and plans to file its appeal before July 27.

Why it matters: A city appealing a federal land-use approval it says it was never consulted on could set a precedent for how BLM handles data-centre siting on public land nationwide.

Google's $9 billion Chesterfield County, Virginia data centre open house draws hundreds

PolicyConstruction

Hundreds of residents attended back-to-back open houses on July 15 and 16 to question Google representatives about its three planned Chesterfield County campuses — Project Peanut, Project Skye and Project Loch — a combined $9 billion investment spanning nearly 1,500 acres, 12 On Your Side reported. 11

Georgia regulators open probe into Georgia Power's data centre pricing methodology

EnergyPolicy

The Georgia Public Service Commission voted unanimously to open a review of how Georgia Power prices electricity for data centres and other large industrial customers, ordering the utility to document its hourly pricing using July 2025 as a test case, Rough Draft Atlanta reported. Commission staff have warned the current structure could push average residential costs up by as much as 11% a month by 2028 if a gap between data-centre rates and actual grid impact is being passed on to households; Georgia Power's responses are due August 10.

Why it matters: A regulator-ordered, hour-by-hour audit of how a utility prices data-centre power could become the template other states use to test whether residential ratepayers are subsidizing AI buildouts.

13

Fayetteville, Georgia data centre investigated over unauthorized water usage

Policy

A QTS data centre on a 600-acre campus in Fayetteville, about 22 miles south of Atlanta, came under county investigation after a nearby resident's report of low water pressure led officials to allege the facility had unauthorized water-line connections and owed roughly $147,000, the Atlanta Tribune reported July 16. The dispute has simmered for more than a year since the initial complaint.

Why it matters: A billing dispute escalating into a formal water-usage investigation illustrates how thin the line has become between routine utility disputes and full-blown regulatory scrutiny of data-centre resource use.

14

Nebius, CoreWeave and IREN shares tumble on report of Meta's in-house cloud ambitions

FinanceTech

Nebius shares fell as much as 13% and CoreWeave and IREN dropped roughly 15% in trading July 16 after a Bloomberg report that Meta is building an in-house "Meta Compute" cloud business to sell excess AI capacity, raising fears hyperscalers could compete with rather than lease from neocloud providers, 24/7 Wall St. reported. Despite the selloff, Nebius retains a $2 billion Nvidia warrant investment and up to $27 billion in combined Meta infrastructure commitments, while CoreWeave carries a $99.4 billion revenue backlog including $21 billion from Meta.

Why it matters: A single report about Meta building its own cloud business wiping out billions in neocloud market value shows how dependent the sector's high valuations remain on hyperscalers staying customers rather than becoming competitors.
