BlackRock markets $12 billion-plus bond sale to finance Meta's El Paso AI campus

FinanceEnergy

BlackRock is looking to sell more than $12 billion in bonds through a holding company for its 80% stake in "Project Sopaipilla," a Meta Platforms data-centre campus in El Paso, Texas, with Meta holding the remaining 20%. JPMorgan and Morgan Stanley are running fixed-income investor calls this week ahead of pricing early next week; Meta has reportedly raised its investment in the El Paso site from $1.5 billion to $10 billion, targeting roughly 1 gigawatt of capacity by 2028. The sale draws on capital from BlackRock's recent $12.5 billion Global Infrastructure Partners and $12 billion HPS Investment Partners acquisitions.

Why it matters: One of the largest single-asset data-centre bond sales on record shows how deeply Wall Street's biggest asset managers are now embedding themselves in AI infrastructure financing, right as demand for that same debt shows signs of strain.

2

PJM ran its first real-world test of a data-centre backup-generator emergency tool

EnergyPolicy

Grid operator PJM disclosed it issued "Transmission Security – Emergency Use of Backup-Generator" warnings on July 2 and 3 as an early-July heat wave pushed demand to an all-time unrestricted peak of 168,158 MW, following a DOE Section 202(c) emergency order. The procedure lets transmission owners mobilize backup generation from large customers, including data centres, within 15 minutes during grid emergencies, but PJM's Independent Market Monitor said the warnings were precautionary and no backup generators were ultimately dispatched.

Why it matters: This was the first live test of a mechanism specifically built to lean on AI data centres' own backup power during grid emergencies — a preview of the tool utilities may increasingly reach for as large loads strain summer peaks.

Bond investors push back as AI-related debt heads toward $570 billion

Finance

Global AI-related debt issuance is on pace to hit roughly $570 billion in 2026, but demand is cooling: hyperscaler bond cover ratios — investor orders per dollar of bonds offered — have fallen from nearly 5x in February to below 2x in July, a sign investors may soon demand wider spreads. A growing share of the financing is also shifting into less-transparent private credit and off-balance-sheet structures, which Morgan Stanley estimates could reach $800 billion for data centres through 2028.

Why it matters: Softening demand for hyperscaler bonds, arriving just as issuance accelerates, is the clearest signal yet that public debt markets are starting to question how much more AI infrastructure spending they can absorb.

PowerPlay AI unveils 400MW behind-the-meter data centre joint venture near Abilene, Texas

EnergyConstruction

PowerPlay AI announced a joint venture with an unnamed, Nasdaq-listed neocloud partner for an initial 400-megawatt behind-the-meter AI data centre in the greater Abilene, Texas area, following site feasibility studies, land assembly and infrastructure assessment. EnergyFinance

6

EPA proposal would strip public comment from data centres' air pollution permits

Policy

The EPA has proposed eliminating public notice and comment requirements for "minor source" air permits, a category that covers the large diesel backup generators and turbines many data centres rely on, with a public hearing scheduled for July 22 and comments due August 21. Environmental groups including the Sierra Club say the change would let data centres and other polluters build out generation capacity with far less transparency and community input.

Why it matters: Minor-source permits are exactly the loophole watchdogs say data centres already use to add diesel and gas generation with minimal scrutiny — removing public comment entirely would remove one of the last checks communities have.

Leon County, Texas rejects Crusoe's tax-abatement bid for proposed $34 billion data centre

PolicyConstruction

Leon County commissioners rejected an initial tax-abatement application from Crusoe Technologies for a proposed $34 billion data centre, citing an incomplete submission, as more than 200 residents packed the meeting to oppose the project. Silicon photonics emerges as core infrastructure for AI data-centre interconnects

Tech

Following OFC 2026, industry analysis points to silicon photonics and co-packaged optics (CPO) as the leading fix for the "memory wall" bottleneck choking large AI clusters, replacing copper signaling with laser-based, chip-integrated optical links that cut latency and heat. Vendors across the stack are racing to commercialize the technology as thousands-of-accelerator clusters increasingly hit the physical limits of electrical interconnects.

Why it matters: As rack densities climb toward the megawatt range, interconnect — not just compute or cooling — is becoming a first-order constraint on how large an AI cluster can actually get built.

Indianapolis council committee advances data-centre moratorium 10-3

Policy

The Indianapolis City-County Council's Metropolitan and Economic Development Committee voted 10-3 along party lines to advance a moratorium on new data-centre approvals running through the end of 2027. The proposal now heads to the full council for a vote on August 10.

Why it matters: Indianapolis would become one of the largest US cities to pause data-centre approvals outright, adding a major Midwest market to the growing list of local governments choosing to hit pause rather than negotiate case by case.

10

China's Z.ai completes 1GW data centre built entirely on domestic chips

Tech

Chinese AI firm Z.ai completed a 1-gigawatt data centre using more than 10,000 domestically made semiconductors — reportedly the largest such facility built by a Chinese AI company — and began partial operation to train its GLM model. The company also completed its acquisition of Zhongke Jiahe, whose software helps run the same AI models efficiently across different Chinese-made chips, including those from Huawei and Cambricon; Z.ai's shares jumped as much as 31% on the news.

Why it matters: A gigawatt-scale cluster running entirely on non-Nvidia silicon is a concrete data point in the export-control debate, showing China's domestic chip ecosystem is closer to supporting frontier-scale training than US policymakers may have assumed.
