1

Valar Atomics and Nvidia power AI chips directly from a startup nuclear reactor in Utah

TechEnergy

At a demonstration event in Utah on July 1, Valar Atomics and Nvidia showed Nvidia Blackwell chips running on power generated by Valar's Ward250 reactor, which went critical on June 18 — the first time a nuclear startup has produced power at all, let alone fed it directly to AI compute. The companies are now scoping a 30-megawatt, near-zero-water data centre pairing Valar's reactor with Nvidia's water-efficient direct-liquid-cooling design.

Why it matters: This is the first real-world proof point that new nuclear and AI compute can be co-located and paired at small scale — a template hyperscalers have been chasing on paper for two years but hadn't yet demonstrated running.

TechConstruction

GE Vernova pitches onsite gas-turbine microgrids to bypass data centre grid queues

TechEnergy

In a July 6 technical brief, GE Vernova laid out four power configurations — from standalone aeroderivative gas turbines to full renewable-thermal hybrid microgrids — that let data centre operators deploy power in weeks rather than the years typical of grid interconnection queues, with pathways to hydrogen co-firing or carbon capture for lower-carbon operation.

Why it matters: As interconnection backlogs stretch past 2030 in many US markets, onsite generation is shifting from a stopgap to the default design choice for gigawatt-scale AI campuses.

Digital Realty buys 1,440 acres and locks in 600MW for new Kansas City hyperscale campus

FinanceEnergy

Digital Realty acquired a 1,440-acre powered site at Kansas's Astra Enterprise Park for roughly $475 million and signed an Energy Service Agreement with utility Evergy for 600MW of power by early 2028, scalable to 2GW — while separately acquiring investment firm Columbia Capital for about $485 million (announced late June).

Why it matters: Securing land, a signed power agreement and an investment platform in one move shows the largest colocation operators are now racing to lock up scarce powered land ahead of the interconnection crunch, not after it.

5

Virginia becomes first US state to bill data centres by the kilowatt-hour

PolicyEnergy

Virginia's new $0.011-per-kWh electricity consumption tax on data centres took effect July 1 — applying to utility-supplied and self-generated power alike — and is projected to raise about $600 million a year, roughly a 10% increase in effective electricity costs for a facility running continuously. The tax expires in 2028 absent renewal.

Why it matters: As the country's largest data centre market, Virginia's per-kWh model will be the test case every other state weighing a similar levy watches before writing its own bill.

Finance

Realty Income formed a programmatic joint venture with Cloud Capital and an unnamed global institutional investor on June 30, seeding it with three northern Virginia data centre assets valued at over $6 billion under 15-to-20-year triple-net leases with investment-grade hyperscale tenants. Why it matters: A REIT best known for retail real estate building a dedicated data centre investment vehicle signals institutional capital now views hyperscale leases as a core, not niche, real estate asset class.

7

Hamilton-owned corporation's secret NDA over proposed Ontario AI data centre draws scrutiny

CanadaPolicy

Hamilton Enterprises Holding Corporation, wholly owned by the City of Hamilton, has signed a non-disclosure agreement with Slate Asset Management over its proposed AI data centre on Hamilton's former Stelco steelmaking lands — muzzling city representatives from sharing details with council, it emerged publicly for the first time July 2. Slate wants to build what it calls the country's first "publicly owned AI Factory," hosting data from Canadian universities.

Why it matters: A city-owned corporation withholding information about a major AI project from its own council — even as Microsoft publicly ends its NDA practice with municipalities — sharpens the transparency fight already reshaping how Canadian cities negotiate data centre deals.

8

Prince William supervisors unanimously reject 43-million-square-foot Dulles South data centre campus

ConstructionPolicy

Following a five-hour public hearing and over local outcry, the Prince William Board of County Supervisors voted 8-0 on July 7 to deny a comprehensive plan amendment that would have reclassified roughly 1,940 acres — some 252 parcels — from agricultural and mixed-use zoning to industrial, killing the proposed "Dulles Cloud South" campus. County staff had recommended denial over impacts to rural character, groundwater and the environment.

Why it matters: A unanimous rejection of a project this size, in the country's largest data centre market, is the clearest signal yet that even Virginia's most data-centre-friendly jurisdictions now have a ceiling — echoing the fate of the neighboring PW Digital Gateway project.

Prince George's County extends data centre moratorium to two years

Policy

The Prince George's County Council voted July 7 to extend its pause on new hyperscale data centre applications to two years, following a contentious hearing where supporters and opponents clashed over utility rates, environmental impact, jobs and economic benefit. The moratorium can lift early if the county adopts a zoning framework specifying where and how data centres may be built.

Why it matters: A two-year pause in a major DC-suburban county — not a one-year stopgap — shows local governments increasingly see slow, deliberate zoning overhauls, not quick fixes, as the only durable answer to the data centre boom.

Policy

Ahead of the July 7 Clark County Commission meeting, the Sierra Club's Toiyabe Chapter held a press conference and released an eight-page policy proposal calling for a data centre moratorium, tighter siting rules away from homes and schools, and mandatory closed-loop cooling. Why it matters: Southern Nevada's fast-growing data centre corridor joining the national regulation wave shows the backlash has spread well beyond the traditional East Coast and Midwest hotspots.

11

Albany Common Council backs New York's statewide data centre moratorium bill

Policy

The Albany Common Council voted 13-0 on July 6 to formally back the Responsible Data Center Development Act (S10642), the statewide one-year moratorium on permits for data centres over 20MW that has sat on Gov. Kathy Hochul's desk since passing the legislature. The vote followed news that the former Kenwood Convent site in Albany is being eyed for a data centre.

Why it matters: A state capital's own council endorsing the moratorium adds political pressure on Hochul just as she weighs signing what would be the first statewide data centre moratorium in the US.

New Albany, Indiana advances one-year data centre moratorium to final vote

Policy

The New Albany City Council advanced Mayor Jeff Gahan's proposed one-year moratorium on new data centres to a final vote on July 6, following a Plan Commission recommendation. If approved July 16, the ordinance would block any new facility, or multi-building campus, exceeding 100,000 square feet.

Why it matters: Indiana already has nearly a third of its counties restricting data centres — New Albany's move shows the state's cities, not just rural counties, are now joining the pushback.

Policy

Policy

Lakeland held its first public hearing July 6 on a proposed 12-month moratorium on data centres and large electric loads over 50 megawatts a month, prompted by resident opposition to the 600,000-square-foot "Project Swan" facility. Why it matters: Even a project developers insist is "just" cloud storage, not an AI facility, is now enough to trigger a moratorium fight — showing the backlash has outrun distinctions the industry once thought mattered.

North Mankato, Minnesota debates one-year data centre moratorium

Policy

North Mankato City Council discussed a proposed 12-month citywide moratorium on new or expanded data centres at a work session this week, with no formal vote taken. The city attorney estimated a follow-up impact study, covering water, power, noise and lighting, could cost $5,000 to $50,000.

Why it matters: Council members worried the moratorium's broad definition could inadvertently snag existing local employers, illustrating how hard it is for smaller cities to write data centre rules narrowly enough to hit only the intended target.

19

Bell County, Texas residents demand end to data centre tax abatements

Policy

Residents pressed Bell County commissioners on July 7 to scrap tax abatements for data centres entirely, arguing the incentives aren't needed to attract projects. Commissioners countered that without zoning authority outside city limits, abatements are one of the few tools they have to impose any conditions at all; a revised policy is due by September 8.

Why it matters: The fight reframes the incentive debate — in unzoned Texas counties, tax abatements aren't just a subsidy question but the only real lever governments have over how projects get built.

20

AI demand pushing data centre construction into a global skilled-labor shortage, report warns

Construction

A Turner & Townsend report published July 8 found data centres are now "the most constrained sector globally" for contractor capacity, with over 70% of 112 markets analyzed showing tightening or overstretched capacity — North America posting the highest regional labor costs of any region studied.

Why it matters: Power and permitting get most of the attention, but this report is a reminder that a shortage of skilled tradespeople could throttle the AI buildout just as hard as grid capacity does.
