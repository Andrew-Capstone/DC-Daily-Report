Meta to spend over $13 billion on its first Canadian data centre in Alberta

CanadaEnergyTech

Meta announced July 8 it will build a 1-gigawatt AI data centre on 1,750 acres in Sturgeon County, north of Edmonton — its first facility in Canada and one of the largest private investments in Canadian history. Pembina Pipeline, Morgan Stanley Infrastructure Partners and Kineticor are separately building a $4.6 billion natural gas plant to supply it, while Meta says the campus itself will run closed-loop, dry-cooled liquid cooling to eliminate operational water use.

Why it matters: It's the largest single AI infrastructure investment ever made in Canada, instantly making Alberta a continental hub for hyperscale AI compute and Western Canadian natural gas demand.

2

Nvidia's Rubin generation becomes first 100% liquid-cooled AI architecture, cutting water use to near zero

TechEnergy

Nvidia detailed a closed-loop liquid-cooling design for its Rubin-generation AI infrastructure — every chip and networking component cooled by liquid with no fans anywhere in the system, running coolant at 45°C to reject heat via outdoor dry coolers instead of water-hungry chillers. The company says the design cuts cooling-related energy use by up to 40% and can save a 50MW facility more than $4 million a year; mass production ships this autumn.

Why it matters: Water consumption has become one of the AI buildout's biggest community flashpoints — a mainstream architecture that needs almost none of it could defuse a large share of the local opposition driving this year's moratorium wave.

3

Power prices triple on PJM as heat wave and data centre demand collide

EnergyPolicy

A brutal East Coast heat dome pushed PJM Interconnection's electricity demand to roughly 163 gigawatts on July 2, just short of the grid's 2006 all-time record, as the Department of Energy issued emergency orders authorizing PJM to curtail data centres and waive pollution limits for power plants through July 3. PJM ultimately didn't invoke its authority to force data centres onto backup generators, but power prices tripled across the grid during the peak.

Why it matters: The nation's largest grid operator coming within a few hundred megawatts of an all-time demand record — and needing emergency federal authority in reserve — is the clearest real-time evidence yet that data centre load is now a genuine grid-reliability issue, not just a planning abstraction.

Nvidia's next-gen Kyber rack system delayed to 2028 on manufacturing snags

Tech

Nvidia's Kyber rack-scale architecture, meant to support 2027's Rubin Ultra chips by connecting 144 chips per rack, has slipped to 2028 after manufacturing problems with a specialized multi-layer circuit board, CNBC reported July 6. Plans for a smaller backup configuration have reportedly been scrapped after cloud customers pushed back on its complexity, and the larger NVL576 multi-rack system is also likely delayed.

Why it matters: A stumble in Nvidia's own rack roadmap — not just chip supply — hands AMD, Google TPUs and custom silicon vendors a rare opening just as hyperscalers plan multi-year capacity around Nvidia's cadence.

Digital Realty buys out Blackstone's $7.8B stake in three Northern Virginia data centres

Finance

Digital Realty agreed to acquire Blackstone-affiliated funds' interest in three fully leased Northern Virginia data centres — two in Manassas, one on its Digital Dulles campus in Sterling — totaling 288MW of IT capacity and valued at $7.8 billion gross. Digital Realty is paying $3.5 billion for Blackstone's blended 64% stake, split between $1.2 billion cash and $2.3 billion in stock, with the deal closing June 30.

Why it matters: Consolidating full ownership of fully leased, investment-grade assets rather than chasing new land shows Digital Realty betting that owning proven, already-stabilized capacity is now as valuable as building new.

TeraWulf signs 20-year, $19B Anthropic lease at Kentucky data campus

FinanceConstruction

TeraWulf announced July 6 a 20-year lease with Anthropic at its Justified Data campus in Hawesville, Kentucky, covering roughly 401MW of critical IT load and generating an estimated $19 billion in contracted lease revenue, with initial capacity live in second-half 2027. FinanceTech

Finance

Canada's data centre capacity could soar to 20GW in planned projects, government document shows

Canada

A federal government document reported July 8 shows Canada currently has about 337 megawatts of operating AI data centre capacity, but more than 20 gigawatts of projects are under planning or development — the majority of it concentrated in Alberta. Opposition to new projects is also spreading, including a proposed moratorium motion under debate at Hamilton city council over a harbourfront campus proposal.

Why it matters: A 60-fold gap between operating and planned capacity shows Canada's data centre boom is still almost entirely on paper — meaning the power, water and community fights that have consumed the US this year are only just beginning north of the border.

13

Parasail pairs Nvidia GPUs with d-Matrix inference chips for 10x faster token generation

Tech

Cloud provider Parasail announced July 8 it is deploying d-Matrix's Corsair inference accelerators alongside Nvidia Hopper and Blackwell GPUs, splitting AI inference workloads so Nvidia chips handle compute-heavy prefill while d-Matrix's memory-integrated chips handle latency-sensitive decode — claiming up to 10x faster, cheaper token generation. The companies call it one of the first commercial-scale examples of this kind of heterogeneous, disaggregated inference architecture.

Why it matters: Mixing chip architectures within a single inference pipeline, rather than standardizing on one vendor, points to a more fragmented and efficiency-driven future for data centre hardware as inference costs become the industry's next battleground.

EnergyConstruction

15

Planned Texas data centre gas plants could emit more greenhouse gases than many countries

PolicyEnergy

An Environmental Integrity Project report found Texas has 32 proposed gas-fired power plants tied to data centres — the largest concentration of any state — that could collectively emit more than 287 million tons of greenhouse gases a year, out of nearly 662 million tons nationally across 74 planned plants. Nearly 90% of the proposed plants nationwide sit in counties with below-average life expectancy, and 40% of nearby residents are low-income.

Why it matters: Quantifying the emissions of the industry's preferred grid-bypass solution — onsite gas — gives opponents a concrete climate and public-health argument just as gas turbines become the default choice for new AI campuses.

Nearly a third of Indiana counties have now moved to restrict data centres

Policy

Indiana University's Environmental Resilience Institute found that, halfway through 2026, 11 Indiana counties have adopted data centre ordinances, at least 17 have temporary moratoriums, and two — Marshall and Cass — have banned new facilities outright, WFYI reported July 6. In Indianapolis, the Metropolitan Development Commission voted 5-3 on July 1 to advance new zoning rules despite residents asking for a pause on approvals first.

Why it matters: Indiana crossing the one-third threshold of counties with some form of restriction shows the moratorium wave has moved from a coastal phenomenon to a genuinely nationwide, Midwest-centered pattern.

17

Santa Fe County, New Mexico adopts pre-emptive 18-month data centre moratorium

Policy

Santa Fe County commissioners voted unanimously July 2 to adopt an 18-month moratorium on data centre development, extended from a proposed 12 months and lowering the regulatory threshold from 100 megawatts to just 1 megawatt. The county currently has no data centres proposed; commissioners said they wanted rules in place before, not after, a project arrives — the first such pre-emptive moratorium in New Mexico.

Why it matters: A county with zero pending projects choosing to write the rulebook in advance marks a tactical shift from reactive local opposition to proactive zoning — a template other jurisdictions may copy before developers even show up.

18

Ohio's ConserveOhio data centre ban petition tops 105,000 signatures

Policy

ConserveOhio, a bipartisan grassroots campaign for a state constitutional amendment banning data centres over 25 megawatts, has collected more than 105,000 signatures — 25% of the 420,142 needed — mobilizing over 1,000 volunteers across all 88 Ohio counties in three months, Salon reported July 9. Having missed the deadline for this year's ballot, organizers are now aiming for a November 2027 statewide vote.

Why it matters: A citizen-led constitutional ban gathering real momentum in a top-six data centre state — rather than a local council moratorium — would mark a significant escalation from zoning-level pushback to a permanent statewide restriction.

Broomfield, Colorado unanimously approves 18-month data centre moratorium

Policy

Broomfield City Council voted 9-0 on July 7 to pass an 18-month moratorium halting new land-use applications, site plans and permits for data centres with a projected load of 10 megawatts or more. Council members cited power demand, water use and community impact concerns in a region already dealing with infrastructure strain.

Why it matters: A unanimous vote in the Denver metro area shows the moratorium wave has now reached the Mountain West's fastest-growing tech corridor, not just the traditional East Coast and Midwest hotspots.

20

Crossville, Tennessee weighs data centre moratorium after crypto-mining noise complaints

Policy

A round-the-clock cryptocurrency mining facility in Crossville, Tennessee is drawing complaints from neighboring businesses over constant noise and heat, even though it measures under the city's 90-decibel threshold, WVLT reported July 7. Mayor RJ Crawford said the city is now considering a two-year moratorium on new data centres while it drafts "common sense" regulation to protect essential services like banks and hospitals from future disruption.

Why it matters: A single noisy crypto facility triggering a citywide moratorium debate shows how thin the tolerance for data centre neighbors has become — before an actual AI hyperscale campus has even arrived.
