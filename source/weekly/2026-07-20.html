Data Centre Weekly Overview

Weekly Overview

July 14 – 20, 2026 · North America focus
Week in review: Canada anchored the week's biggest headline: Meta broke ground on its first Canadian data centre, a $13 billion, 1-gigawatt campus in Sturgeon County, Alberta, even as the province opened public comment on a province-wide "bring-your-own-generation" bridging framework and a Canadian Press analysis found the country's data-centre pipeline nearing 20 gigawatts amid building public opposition. Technology broke new ground on multiple fronts — Nvidia's fully liquid-cooled Rubin architecture, Aalo Atomics' criticality-achieving microreactor, Samsung Heavy Industries' 2028 floating data centre, and Meta's in-house "Iris" AI chip entering production in September — while TSMC pledged a further $100 billion for Arizona fabs, pushing its total US commitment to $265 billion. Energy remained the connective tissue elsewhere: Meta's Louisiana "Hyperion" campus ballooned to 5GW and $50 billion, Elon Musk quietly bought a $1 billion mobile gas-turbine fleet to power xAI, and fresh fuel-cell and solar deals kept flowing to Oracle, Nebius and Google even as Oracle's credit downgrade collided with new Wisconsin collateral rules. Regulation cut sharply against the buildout too, with New York becoming the first US state to impose a statewide hyperscale moratorium. Capital markets stayed enormous regardless — Switch lined up a potential $80 billion IPO and Digital Realty closed a $7.8 billion Blackstone buyout — underscoring how much money is still chasing AI infrastructure even as the political and regulatory backlash intensifies.
1

Meta breaks ground on $13 billion Sturgeon County, Alberta data centre — its first in Canada

CanadaEnergyConstruction

Meta officially broke ground on a CAD $13 billion, 1-gigawatt AI campus in Sturgeon County, Alberta on July 8 — its first Canadian data centre and 33rd worldwide — with Premier Danielle Smith and Meta data-centre VP Gary Demasi at the ceremony. The project is expected to employ up to 3,000 workers at peak construction, generate roughly $250 million a year in royalties, taxes and fees for Alberta, and will use a closed-loop, dry-cooling system with no operational water draw, matched with 100% clean and renewable electricity.

Why it matters: The largest private-sector investment of its kind in Canadian history, paired with a zero-operational-water cooling commitment, sets the template Alberta and other provinces will use to court the next wave of hyperscale investment.

2

Nvidia's Rubin-generation data centres achieve 100% liquid cooling, near-zero water use

TechEnergy

Nvidia says its Rubin-generation AI architecture is the first to cool every chip and networking component entirely by liquid in a closed loop with no fans, running coolant at 45°C (113°F) to passively reject heat. The design uses a 75% water, 25% propylene-glycol coolant mix, cuts data-centre water use from roughly 2.6 million gallons per megawatt annually to near zero, and lets systems fit in 2 rack units instead of 6, with mass production and shipments beginning this autumn.

Why it matters: A 100%-liquid-cooled reference design that all but eliminates data-centre water consumption tackles the industry's most politically explosive resource fight just as communities nationwide cite water use to block new projects.

3

Meta's Louisiana "Hyperion" data centre expands to 5GW as investment tops $50 billion

EnergyFinance

Meta confirmed its Hyperion supercluster in Richland Parish, Louisiana will scale to 5GW of capacity with total investment exceeding $50 billion, up sharply from the $27 billion disclosed last October and the original $10 billion estimate, CNBC reported July 13. Blue Owl Capital holds an 80% stake in the joint venture, and the project — backed by a 20-year state sales-tax exemption — has already awarded more than $1.6 billion in contracts to local businesses.

Why it matters: A fivefold cost escalation on a single campus illustrates how fast AI infrastructure budgets are growing even for the best-capitalized hyperscalers, intensifying scrutiny of the tax breaks and power commitments underwriting these megaprojects.

TSMC pledges additional $100 billion for Arizona, pushing total US commitment to $265 billion

TechFinance

TSMC announced at its July 16 Q2 earnings conference in Taipei that it will invest an additional $100 billion in Arizona for at least four more 2-nanometer-and-below fabs plus advanced packaging plants, bringing its total pledged US investment to $265 billion across as many as 10 fabs. The chipmaker posted a record quarterly profit on surging AI demand and raised its full-year 2026 capex budget to $60-64 billion from a prior $52-56 billion.

Why it matters: A $100 billion top-up for Arizona chip capacity shows the AI data-centre boom is now reshaping where the world's most advanced silicon gets made, not just where it gets deployed.

5

Aalo Atomics' test reactor reaches criticality, fourth DOE-backed microreactor to hit July 4 goal

EnergyTech

Aalo Atomics' 10-MWe sodium-cooled Aalo-X Critical Test Reactor reached criticality at Idaho National Laboratory on July 4, becoming the fourth advanced reactor to do so under the Department of Energy's Reactor Pilot Program deadline, Business Wire reported. The test reactor shares full-scale core components with Aalo's commercial "Aalo Pod" design, which the Austin-based company is positioning as a power source built specifically for on-site data-centre deployment.

Why it matters: A working microreactor design purpose-built to co-locate with data centres shows the nuclear-for-AI thesis moving from paper agreements to physical hardware years ahead of the utility-scale SMRs hyperscalers have already committed billions to.

6

Meta to put in-house "Iris" AI chip into production in September, targeting 14GW of compute

TechFinance

Meta plans to begin manufacturing its custom AI chip, code-named Iris, in September as part of the Meta Training and Inference Accelerator (MTIA) family designed in-house with Broadcom and built by TSMC, Reuters and CNBC reported July 9. The chip is meant to supplement, not replace, Nvidia and AMD GPUs as Meta aims to double its computing capacity from 7GW in 2026 to 14GW in 2027, backed by a raised 2026 capex forecast of up to $145 billion.

Why it matters: A hyperscaler moving to a roughly six-month, four-generation custom-silicon cadence signals AI infrastructure economics are shifting away from near-total Nvidia dependency, a trend that will reshape chip demand and margins across the industry.

7

SpaceX quietly rents Colossus GPU capacity to Anthropic and Google for ~$26 billion a year, eyes Pentagon deal

TechFinance

SpaceX is renting spare Nvidia GPU capacity from its Memphis-area Colossus and Colossus II data centres to Anthropic (325,000 GPUs for $1.25 billion a month) and Google (110,000 GPUs for $920 million a month), Fortune reported July 19 — a combined run rate of roughly $26 billion a year. The company is separately in talks to supply the Pentagon with AI data-centre capacity potentially worth billions, following a $3.2 billion AI-segment 2025 revenue base that still ran a $6.4 billion operating loss.

Why it matters: A rocket company turning idle AI compute into a business line rivaling a mid-size cloud provider — with the Pentagon as a prospective customer — shows how thoroughly GPU capacity itself has become the asset class investors are pricing SpaceX's $1.8 trillion valuation against.

8

Samsung Heavy Industries targets 2028 for floating, barge-based AI data centre

TechEnergy

Samsung Heavy Industries is developing a purpose-built 50MW barge data centre — combining server halls, onboard power generation and LNG fuel storage — with an initial "nearshore" version drawing partly on land-based grid power before fully offshore deployment, TheNextWeb reported July 8. The approach is designed to sidestep three converging constraints: land scarcity, multi-year grid interconnection queues, and freshwater cooling demand, using seawater for cooling instead.

Why it matters: A commercial timeline for offshore compute shows land and grid bottlenecks are pushing the industry toward genuinely novel siting models, not just further out into rural counties.

9

New York becomes first US state to impose a statewide hyperscale data centre moratorium

PolicyEnergy

Governor Kathy Hochul signed an executive order on July 14 pausing state environmental permits for new hyperscale data centres for up to a year while New York develops a Generic Environmental Impact Statement and stronger regulatory standards, according to the governor's office. The order also directs a Community Investment Framework within 60 days for local benefit negotiations, floats a New York Grid Acceleration Fund, and pursues legislation to repeal the state's sales-tax exemption for large data centres.

Why it matters: The first statewide hyperscale moratorium in the country hands every other state legislature a ready-made template for pausing permits while they write their own rules.

Data centre operator Switch lines up potential $10 billion IPO near $80 billion valuation

Finance

Las Vegas-based data-centre operator Switch has tapped Goldman Sachs and JPMorgan to lead a possible IPO as soon as the fourth quarter that could raise up to $10 billion and value the company near $80 billion including debt, Yahoo Finance/Reuters reported July 14. DigitalBridge and IFM Investors took Switch private for $11 billion in 2022; the company, which serves customers including Nvidia and Dell, has also discussed a pre-IPO private raise at a $40 billion-plus valuation.

Why it matters: A potential $80 billion valuation — roughly seven times Switch's 2022 take-private price — would rank among the largest US listings in years and test how much public markets will pay for pure-play AI infrastructure.

11

Alberta opens comment on "bridging" power framework to fast-track data centres onto the grid

CanadaEnergyPolicy

The Alberta Electric System Operator closed public feedback July 13 on a draft "bring-your-own-generation" bridging framework that would grant qualifying data centres up to three years of temporary grid access while their own on-site power comes online, extending well beyond the 1.6-gigawatt temporary pool the province opened for gas-fired projects earlier this month. A finalized version is expected to roll out in August, building on Phase 1 rules that already allocated 1.2 gigawatts of interim capacity to two projects.

Why it matters: Alberta is trying to codify, province-wide, the exact bring-your-own-power bargain the rest of North America is still fighting over case by case — a durable regulatory template could make it the continent's fastest place to get a hyperscale data centre connected.

Elon Musk quietly acquired mobile gas-turbine firm APR Energy for over $1 billion to power xAI

EnergyFinance

Musk acquired Jacksonville-based APR Energy — which operates trailer-mounted gas and diesel turbines capable of generating more than 1 gigawatt and deploying in 15-30 days — in a deal worth over $1 billion completed quietly in May 2026, DCD and Tom's Hardware reported, only surfacing publicly this month via an FTC early-termination notice. The acquisition gives xAI a mobile, grid-independent power fleet it can deploy directly at data-centre sites without waiting on interconnection queues.

Why it matters: Buying an entire mobile power-generation company rather than just contracting for turbines shows Musk treating on-site generation as core infrastructure to own outright, intensifying the gas-turbine emissions fight already surrounding his Memphis Colossus sites.

13

Digital Realty buys out Blackstone's stake in three Northern Virginia data centres for $7.8 billion

Finance

Digital Realty agreed to acquire Blackstone-affiliated funds' interests in three fully leased Northern Virginia data centres totaling 288 megawatts — two in Manassas and one on the Digital Dulles campus in Sterling — at a $7.8 billion gross valuation, paying Blackstone $3.5 billion in cash and stock for its blended 64% stake, per GlobeNewswire. The properties carry 15-year leases to three investment-grade hyperscale customers with 3.6% annual rent escalators, with two facilities stabilizing in 2027 and the third in 2028.

Why it matters: A public REIT paying $7.8 billion for already-leased Virginia capacity underscores how scarce and valuable operating hyperscale assets have become in the country's largest data-centre market.

14

Canadian Press: opposition brewing nationally as Canada's data-centre pipeline nears 20GW

CanadaPolicy

A Canadian Press analysis published July 18 found Canada has 1.6GW of active data-centre capacity against 22.2GW planned or under development as of June — Alberta alone accounting for 92% of the pipeline despite hosting just 3.3% of current facilities — with opposition organizing from Vancouver to Hamilton. A new Leger poll cited in the piece found Canadians split 46% supportive to 37% opposed, while researchers framed data centres as "the physical avatar" of broader public anxiety about AI.

Why it matters: With planned capacity outnumbering active capacity nearly 14-to-1, Canada is entering the same fight the US had last year over siting and power — except concentrated almost entirely in one province's gas-fired grid.

15

Oracle's credit downgrade collides with Wisconsin's new data-centre credit rules

FinancePolicy

S&P Global's July 9 downgrade of Oracle to BBB- — citing "an uncertain path to profitability" amid a $250 billion AI data-centre buildout that burned nearly $24 billion in free cash flow last fiscal year — has collided with a Wisconsin Public Service Commission rule requiring data-centre customers of We Energies to hold a AAA- credit rating or post steep collateral, potentially over $100 million a year. Oracle now falls below that threshold for its Ozaukee County project; We Energies calls the rule an unfair reading of Oracle's finances and has pushed back, while the PSC has declined to reconsider and consumer group Citizens Utility Board is defending the ratepayer protections.

Why it matters: A state utility regulator using credit ratings to force AI hyperscalers to self-insure against their own default risk — rather than leaving local ratepayers exposed — could become the template other states copy as the sector's debt load climbs toward $570 billion.

16

Oracle's $165 billion "Project Jupiter" hits a second New Mexico rejection, clouding Bloom Energy's fuel-cell bet

EnergyFinance

New Mexico regulators rejected Energy Transfer's natural-gas pipeline application for the second time on public-interest grounds, dealing a fresh blow to Oracle's $165 billion, 2.45-gigawatt "Project Jupiter" AI campus, which had already dropped its original gas-plant plan under local pressure over emissions and water use in favor of Bloom Energy solid-oxide fuel cells. That fuel-cell fallback still needs an air permit that remains pending, leaving the project's power source unresolved on two fronts at once. Bloom Energy shares, up 149% year-to-date, now sit roughly 39% below their June peak as investors reassess how quickly the deal can actually close.

Why it matters: When both the gas-pipeline and fuel-cell paths to power a $165 billion campus stall in the same state within months of each other, it is a sharp reminder that regulatory approval, not capital or chips, is now the binding constraint on hyperscale AI buildouts.

Google signs vPPA for full 2.45GW output of Arkansas solar-plus-storage project

Energy

Google agreed to a virtual power purchase agreement covering the entire output of Cypress Creek Renewables' Steel River Energy Center in Mississippi County, Arkansas, a $3.5 billion project that will pair 2.45GW of solar with 2.9GWh of battery storage, with a first 1.6GW phase targeted for 2029. Google said the deal, its second in the state after a 100MW agreement with Treaty Oak, supplies the broader grid rather than a dedicated facility.

Why it matters: Locking in an entire solar-plus-storage project's output gives Cypress Creek the revenue certainty to finance construction, a financing pattern increasingly required to get net-new generation built fast enough for AI demand.

Oaktree and IDF commit $1.7 billion to Bloom Energy fuel cells for Nebius's US build-out

EnergyFinance

Industrial Development Funding and Oaktree Capital Management announced a $1.7 billion project investment on July 16 to deploy Bloom Energy solid-oxide fuel cells as behind-the-meter power for Nebius's US AI data centres, extending an IDF-Bloom partnership that now spans more than $2.6 billion. 19

QTS and Lancium unveil $10 billion, 1GW, 11-building AI campus in Hall County, Texas

ConstructionEnergy

QTS Data Centers and Lancium announced plans to invest $10 billion building up to 11 data centres across roughly 465 acres near Turkey in Hall County, Texas, anchored by a 1-gigawatt grid connection, DCD reported. Lancium and QTS will fund 100% of the energy infrastructure themselves, including on-site solar and battery storage, and the closed-loop cooling design draws all water from on-site wells rather than the town's municipal supply.

Why it matters: A hyperscale operator and power developer footing the entire grid and water bill upfront, rather than leaning on a small Texas town's utilities, is becoming the template rural counties are starting to demand before they'll say yes.

20

Keel Infrastructure advances 96MW Sherbrooke, Québec data centre project

CanadaEnergy

Keel Infrastructure received Sherbrooke municipal approval and an agreement with Hydro-Sherbrooke to consolidate three existing Bitcoin-mining sites into a single 96MW campus, reclassifying the power from cryptocurrency mining to high-performance computing and AI use without requesting additional capacity, according to a July 15 report. The company also signed a purchase agreement for land roughly 100 miles east of Montreal, with the deal's closing expected in the first quarter of 2027 pending site and municipal approvals.

Why it matters: Converting already-powered crypto-mining sites into AI data centres sidesteps the grid-interconnection queues slowing new projects across Canada, offering a faster and less contentious path to HPC capacity.
