Data Centre Weekly Overview

Weekly Overview

July 7 – 13, 2026 · North America focus
Week in review: Canada supplied the week's biggest story: Meta broke ground on a $13 billion CAD, 1GW data centre in Sturgeon County, Alberta — its largest facility outside the US — while the Pembina-led consortium building its dedicated $4.6B gas plant reached final investment decision and Hamilton's council advanced what would be Canada's first city-wide data centre moratorium. Energy was the connective tissue everywhere else: FERC ordered all six major US grid operators to rewrite interconnection rules for gigawatt-scale loads, a PJM heat wave pushed power prices to record highs, and nuclear, hydrogen and fuel-cell power for AI campuses kept advancing through Aalo Atomics' microreactor, GridMarket's $22.5B nuclear pipeline and EQT's purchase of Copia Power. On the technology side, Nvidia's fully liquid-cooled Rubin architecture and QuiX Quantum's photonic HPC chip pointed to two different frontiers of AI infrastructure design, while SK Hynix's record $26.5B IPO and Micron's $250B US investment underscored how much capital is chasing the chip supply chain. Financing stayed enormous — Anthropic's $19B TeraWulf lease, Digital Realty's $7.8B Blackstone buyout — even as local opposition scored its biggest win of the year when Prince William County, Virginia unanimously killed a 43-million-square-foot data centre campus.
1

Meta breaks ground on its first Canadian data centre, a $13B CAD campus in Alberta

CanadaEnergyConstruction

Meta broke ground July 8 on a new 1GW, AI-optimized data centre in Sturgeon County, Alberta — its first facility in Canada and 33rd globally — representing an investment of more than CAD $13 billion. The project will employ roughly 3,000 workers at peak construction, use closed-loop cooling with no ongoing water draw, and includes about CAD $60 million for local roads, water systems and community grants.

Why it matters: This is Meta's largest data centre outside the US and a landmark moment for Canada's AI infrastructure ambitions, cementing Alberta's position as the country's hyperscale hub.

2

Pembina-led consortium sanctions $4.6B Greenlight gas plant to power Meta's Alberta campus

CanadaEnergy

Pembina Pipeline confirmed a positive final investment decision, alongside Morgan Stanley Infrastructure Partners and Kineticor Asset Management, on the Greenlight Electricity Centre — a dedicated, behind-the-meter gas-to-power plant that will supply Meta's Sturgeon County data centre under a long-term tolling agreement. Meta won't own the plant itself; at full 1.8GW build-out the campus will draw roughly 150 million cubic feet of natural gas per day.

Why it matters: This is the primary power source behind Meta's landmark Canadian campus, and shows pipeline and infrastructure-fund capital, not just utilities, increasingly building bespoke gas plants to win hyperscale tolling contracts.

3

FERC orders all six US grid operators to justify or rewrite data centre interconnection rules

EnergyPolicy

The Federal Energy Regulatory Commission issued Section 206 show-cause orders to PJM, MISO, SPP, CAISO, ISO-NE and NYISO, directing each to prove its large-load interconnection tariffs are just and reasonable or file reforms addressing gigawatt-scale requests, co-located generation and cost allocation. Formal RTO/ISO responses are due August 17.

Why it matters: This is the federal government's most aggressive move yet to standardize how AI data centres connect to the grid nationwide, and its outcome will shape interconnection speed and cost allocation for every hyperscale project in the country.

4

Nvidia's Rubin generation becomes first 100% liquid-cooled AI architecture, cutting water use to near zero

TechEnergy

Nvidia detailed a closed-loop liquid-cooling design for its Rubin-generation AI infrastructure — every chip and networking component cooled by liquid with no fans anywhere in the system, running coolant at 45°C to reject heat via outdoor dry coolers instead of water-hungry chillers. Nvidia says the design cuts cooling-related energy use by up to 40% and can save a 50MW facility more than $4 million a year; mass production ships this autumn.

Why it matters: Water consumption has become one of the AI buildout's biggest community flashpoints — a mainstream architecture that needs almost none of it could defuse a large share of the local opposition driving this year's moratorium wave.
Nvidia Blog →
5

Valar Atomics and Nvidia power AI chips directly from a startup nuclear reactor in Utah

TechEnergy

Valar Atomics and Nvidia demonstrated Nvidia Blackwell chips running on power generated by Valar's Ward250 reactor, which went critical on June 18 — the first time a nuclear startup has produced power at all, let alone fed it directly to AI compute. The companies are now scoping a 30-megawatt, near-zero-water data centre pairing Valar's reactor with Nvidia's water-efficient cooling.

Why it matters: This is the first real-world proof point that new nuclear and AI compute can be co-located and paired at small scale — a template hyperscalers have been chasing on paper for two years but hadn't yet demonstrated running.

6

QuiX Quantum unveils photonic Dedalo architecture built for HPC data centres

Tech

QuiX Quantum introduced its Dedalo architecture, which it calls the first logical-qubit-based photonic quantum computer built for integration into traditional HPC data centres, using CMOS-compatible silicon-nitride photonic chips that run at room temperature and are compatible with standard fiber-optic telecom gear already inside data centres.

Why it matters: A room-temperature quantum architecture designed to slot into existing HPC facilities, rather than requiring dedicated cryogenic infrastructure, could be the first practical bridge between quantum and classical AI data centre computing.

7

SK Hynix raises $26.5B in the biggest foreign IPO in US history to fund AI memory production

TechFinance

SK Hynix sold 177.9 million American Depositary Shares at $149 each on July 10, raising $26.5 billion in the largest-ever US debut by a non-American company, topping Alibaba's 2014 IPO. The chipmaker, a primary high-bandwidth-memory supplier to Nvidia, will use proceeds for a new South Korean fab, a packaging facility and EUV scanners to address AI-driven memory shortages.

Why it matters: The record-breaking raise shows public capital markets are now directly financing the memory-chip bottleneck underpinning every hyperscale AI data centre buildout, not just private and project debt.
TechCrunch →
8

GridMarket and Deployable Energy strike $22.5B nuclear pipeline for data centres through 2035

EnergyTech

GridMarket and microreactor maker Deployable Energy announced a partnership to commercialize Deployable's Unity Nuclear Battery — which achieved criticality the prior week — across GridMarket's hyperscaler and industrial customer base, targeting 500MW of new nuclear capacity deployed annually from 2030 through 2035, an estimated $145 billion in lifetime contract value.

Why it matters: Pairing a newly critical microreactor design with an existing project-facilitation platform could turn nuclear power for data centres from one-off pilot deals into a repeatable, at-scale product.

9

Aalo Atomics' microreactor reaches criticality, purpose-built for AI data centre power

TechEnergy

Aalo Atomics' Critical Test Reactor achieved initial criticality at Idaho National Laboratory on July 4, becoming the fourth US microreactor to beat a White House deadline requiring at least three advanced reactors to reach criticality by Independence Day — built in under eight months from groundbreaking. Aalo packages its reactors into modular 50MWe "Aalo Pods," with a commercial-scale unit slated to power an on-site data centre by 2027.

Why it matters: A commercial-scale microreactor going from groundbreaking to fission in under eight months shows advanced nuclear is starting to move at a timeline that could actually match AI data centres' power needs.

10

Meta's custom "Iris" AI chip enters production in September, targeting 14GW of compute

Tech

An internal Meta memo shows the company's in-house data centre accelerator, code-named Iris and part of its four-generation MTIA program, will begin manufacturing in September after six weeks of testing turned up no major issues. Meta is co-designing the chip with Broadcom and manufacturing it via TSMC as part of a plan to roughly double computing capacity to 14GW next year.

Why it matters: A working custom silicon program at this scale would meaningfully cut Meta's dependence on Nvidia and AMD and lower the cost per unit of AI compute across its entire data centre fleet.
TechCrunch →
11

EQT buys Copia Power from Carlyle, picking up a 9GW data centre power pipeline

EnergyFinance

Infrastructure investor EQT acquired Copia Power — an energy-and-data-centre developer that co-locates power generation, high-voltage transmission and data centre infrastructure at a single interconnection point — from Carlyle's infrastructure group. Copia operates or is building more than 2.6GW of energy assets and is developing over 9GW of grid-connected data centre capacity backed by 25GW of solar-plus-storage and 7GW of gas generation.

Why it matters: The deal shows infrastructure funds racing to buy entire "power-plus-land" platforms outright rather than just financing individual projects, as behind-the-meter generation becomes the scarcest asset in the AI buildout.

12

Hamilton council votes 15-1 to advance what would be Canada's first city-wide data centre moratorium

CanadaPolicy

Hamilton city council voted 15-1 to advance a proposed year-long, renewable moratorium on new data centres, rejecting a bid to exempt smaller research-focused facilities. A final council vote is scheduled for next month; the pause follows a committee's rejection of Slate Asset Management's plan to carve out part of its harbourfront "Steelport" redevelopment for a data centre campus.

Why it matters: If it passes, Hamilton would become the first Canadian city to adopt a data centre moratorium, a milestone that could embolden other Canadian municipalities watching the US wave of local pauses.

13

Leaked federal document puts Canada's data centre pipeline at 20GW; Ottawa pushes back

CanadaPolicy

A federal presentation obtained by The Canadian Press shows Canada has roughly 337MW of AI data centre capacity operating today against more than 20GW of projects proposed or in planning, most concentrated in Alberta. Industry Minister Evan Solomon's office disputed the figure's significance, saying the national AI strategy projects a far more modest 5.5GW of commercial demand by 2030.

Why it matters: The gap between the headline pipeline number and the government's own demand forecast underscores how speculative much of the announced Canadian buildout still is, even as Alberta races ahead on approvals.

DeepInfra opens its first data centre outside the US in Toronto

CanadaTech

AI inference cloud provider DeepInfra deployed a 1.7MW facility packed with more than 1,000 Nvidia Blackwell B300 GPUs in Toronto — its ninth data centre location overall and first outside the United States, following a $107 million Series B round.

Why it matters: A dedicated inference-cloud operator picking Toronto for its first international site is another sign Canada is becoming a real node in the AI compute map, not just a hyperscaler power play.

15

Micron lifts total US chip investment to $250B, pours first concrete on record New York fab

TechFinanceConstruction

Micron is expanding its domestic investment commitment to roughly $250 billion through 2035 — about $50 billion more than previously planned — and unveiled a $500 million partnership plus 10-year supply agreement with Taiwan's GlobalWafers to expand silicon wafer manufacturing in Texas. The company also confirmed it has poured first concrete at its Clay, New York fab, which it calls the largest semiconductor manufacturing campus in US history.

Why it matters: Securing the upstream silicon-wafer supply chain, not just GPU capacity, is becoming the next constraint hyperscalers and memory makers are racing to lock down as AI data centre demand accelerates.

16

Power prices triple on PJM as heat wave and data centre demand collide

EnergyPolicy

A brutal East Coast heat dome pushed PJM Interconnection's electricity demand to roughly 163 gigawatts, just short of the grid's 2006 all-time record, as the Department of Energy issued emergency orders authorizing PJM to curtail data centres and waive pollution limits for power plants. PJM ultimately didn't force data centres onto backup generators, but power prices tripled across the grid during the peak.

Why it matters: The nation's largest grid operator coming within a few hundred megawatts of an all-time demand record — and needing emergency federal authority in reserve — is the clearest real-time evidence yet that data centre load is now a genuine grid-reliability issue, not just a planning abstraction.

17

Virginia's first-in-the-nation data centre electricity tax takes effect

PolicyEnergy

Virginia's new consumption tax of $0.011 per kilowatt-hour on all electricity used by data centres — including self-generated and behind-the-meter power — took effect July 1, projected to raise roughly $600 million annually while preserving the industry's existing equipment sales-tax exemption. The tax sunsets in 2028 absent renewal.

Why it matters: As the country's largest data centre market, Virginia putting a direct price on power consumption — rather than just capital investment — sets a template other power-constrained states, including Canadian provinces, are already watching.

18

Anthropic signs $19B, 20-year lease with TeraWulf for 401MW Kentucky AI data centre

FinanceEnergy

Anthropic agreed to a 20-year lease worth roughly $19 billion at TeraWulf's Justified Data campus in Hawesville, Kentucky — a 790-acre former aluminum-smelting site with existing power and fiber infrastructure. The campus will scale to 401 MW of critical IT load, with initial capacity live in late 2027 and full build-out by 2028.

Why it matters: A $19B commitment against roughly $3–4B of TeraWulf's own investment shows AI labs are now underwriting entire power-and-compute campuses directly — reshaping who captures value in the buildout.

19

Digital Realty closes $7.8B Blackstone buyout of three Northern Virginia data centres

Finance

Digital Realty closed its acquisition of Blackstone-affiliated funds' interest in three fully leased Northern Virginia data centres — two in Manassas, one on its Digital Dulles campus in Sterling — totaling 288MW of IT capacity and valued at $7.8 billion gross. Digital Realty paid $3.5 billion for Blackstone's blended 64% stake, split between cash and stock.

Why it matters: Consolidating full ownership of fully leased, investment-grade assets rather than chasing new land shows Digital Realty betting that owning proven, already-stabilized capacity is now as valuable as building new.

20

Prince William supervisors unanimously reject 43-million-square-foot Dulles South data centre campus

ConstructionPolicy

Following a five-hour public hearing, the Prince William Board of County Supervisors voted 8-0 to deny a comprehensive plan amendment that would have reclassified roughly 1,940 acres from agricultural and mixed-use zoning to industrial, killing the proposed "Dulles Cloud South" campus. County staff had recommended denial over impacts to rural character, groundwater and the environment.

Why it matters: A unanimous rejection of a project this size, in the country's largest data centre market, is the clearest signal yet that even Virginia's most data-centre-friendly jurisdictions now have a ceiling — echoing the fate of the neighboring PW Digital Gateway project days earlier.
