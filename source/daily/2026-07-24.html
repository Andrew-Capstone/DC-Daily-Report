1

AMD unveils full Instinct MI400 lineup, claims MI500 chips will deliver 1,000x AI performance gain

Tech

At its Advancing AI 2026 event in San Francisco, AMD CEO Lisa Su unveiled the complete Instinct MI400 series: the MI455X, carrying 432GB of HBM4 memory and 320 billion transistors for frontier AI training and inference, and the MI430X, targeted at sovereign AI and HPC workloads. AMD also previewed its MI500 roadmap, claiming it will deliver up to 1,000 times the AI performance of the MI300X, alongside its Helios rackscale platform, now in production for gigawatt-scale deployment; AMD's data-centre revenue hit $5.8 billion in the first quarter, up 57% year-over-year.

Why it matters: A fully fleshed-out MI400 lineup plus a bold MI500 roadmap is AMD's clearest bid yet to lock in gigawatt-scale AI customers who don't want to depend on Nvidia alone.

2

Nvidia details Vera CPU and Vera Rubin rack strategy to challenge AMD and Intel

Tech

Nvidia released full specifications and benchmark details for its custom Vera CPU, the processor at the heart of its next-generation Vera Rubin platform, which pairs it with the Rubin GPU, NVLink 6, ConnectX-9 SuperNIC, BlueField-4 DPU and Spectrum-6 switch. Nvidia VP Ian Buck said Vera Rubin systems have already shipped to major AI companies, with AWS, Google Cloud, Microsoft, OCI, CoreWeave, Lambda, Nebius and Nscale among the first cloud providers deploying Vera Rubin instances in 2026.

Why it matters: By fielding its own custom CPU alongside its GPUs, Nvidia is moving to own even more of the AI server bill of materials, tightening the squeeze on AMD and Intel in data-centre compute.

3

Trump expands "Ratepayer Protection Pledge" to shield consumers from data-centre power costs

EnergyPolicy

President Trump announced an expansion of his Ratepayer Protection Pledge, first signed in March by seven companies, to now include 23 Republican governors and roughly 200 stakeholders covering an estimated 80% of the power distributed to US homes and businesses. The voluntary pledge calls on data centres to bring their own power or pay for the generation and infrastructure they use, invest in local communities, and make backup generation available to communities in emergencies.

Why it matters: A national push to make data centres "pay their own way" for power shows the White House now treats AI-driven electricity costs as a first-tier consumer issue, even though the pledge carries no force of law.

4

Virginia narrowly avoids blackout as 3GW of "Data Center Alley" load drops off grid at once

Energy

A transmission line fault in Northern Virginia's "Data Center Alley" caused roughly 3 gigawatts of data-centre load — about 60 facilities — to automatically switch to backup power between 7:55 and 8am on July 22, triggering a spike in PJM's area control error. It's the largest in a series of similar incidents in the region, following a smaller 1,500MW event in 2024, underscoring how concentrated data-centre load has become a distinct grid-reliability risk.

Why it matters: A near-miss of this scale in the world's densest data-centre market shows grid operators remain exposed to sudden, simultaneous load swings that hyperscale AI campuses can trigger.

5

Alberta electricity industry expects data centres to triple wholesale power prices

CanadaEnergy

Analysis reported by Canada's National Observer found Alberta's electricity industry expects wholesale power prices could triple as the province's grid absorbs a wave of new data-centre demand, with the Alberta Electric System Operator fielding more than 20,000 megawatts of data-centre load requests against current peak demand of only about 12,000 MW. The AESO has capped the grid capacity available to data centres at 1,200 MW while it finalizes longer-term rules for large loads.

Why it matters: A tripling of power prices would land directly on Albertans' bills, turning the data-centre boom the province has been courting into a live affordability fight.

Finance

Alphabet raised its full-year 2026 capital expenditure guidance to a range of $195 billion to $205 billion, up from $180-190 billion previously, after Google Cloud revenue surged and CFO Anat Ashkenazi said the increase reflects accelerating delivery of AI computing capacity to meet demand. Why it matters: Google joining Microsoft, Amazon and Meta in raising already-enormous AI infrastructure budgets confirms hyperscaler capex is still accelerating, not plateauing, years into the AI boom.

9

Oracle faces $7 billion collateral requirement for Wisconsin AI data centre

PolicyFinance

Wisconsin's Public Service Commission upheld rules requiring Oracle to post more than $7 billion in collateral, such as a letter of credit, before utility We Energies will supply power to Oracle's data-centre project in the state — a requirement that could cost Oracle over $100 million a year. The ruling follows S&P Global's July 9 downgrade of Oracle to BBB-, two notches below the credit rating We Energies' tariff requires, adding financial strain as Oracle works to fund its $300 billion compute contract with OpenAI.

Why it matters: A single state utility regulator forcing billions in collateral onto one of the industry's most aggressive builders shows utilities are starting to price in the credit risk of hyperscale AI buildouts, not just the power demand.

12

El Paso City Council unanimously approves new Data Center Policy Framework

Policy

El Paso's City Council unanimously adopted its first Data Center Policy Framework on July 20, the product of nearly six months of research and public engagement, establishing enhanced public review and stricter environmental standards for future hyperscale projects and forgoing local economic incentives for qualifying developments. The move follows months of community pushback over a Meta data centre already under development in the city.

Why it matters: A major Texas city choosing tighter scrutiny and no tax breaks over incentive-driven recruitment offers a template other municipalities can point to as backlash against hyperscale buildouts grows.

Why it matters: Data-centre regulation becoming a bipartisan flashpoint in Texas — the industry's fastest-growing hub — shows political tolerance for unchecked buildout is narrowing even in the most business-friendly states.

Sunbury, Ohio voters to decide 25MW cap that could block $2B Amazon data centre campus

PolicyConstruction

Sunbury's City Council certified a citizen-led charter amendment for the November 3 ballot that would cap any data centre's peak load at 25 megawatts, a threshold that would block the hyperscale facilities now spreading across central Ohio — including a planned $2 billion Amazon Data Services campus in the town's new technology park. Organizers gathered far more signatures than the 174 required to qualify the measure.

Why it matters: A single small-town ballot measure blocking a multibillion-dollar Amazon project shows how much leverage local voters now hold over hyperscale siting decisions, regardless of state or federal policy.

ConstructionEnergy
