1

Texas bets on a 765kV, wires-first grid strategy to unlock AI-scale power

Energy

A new Data Center Knowledge analysis (July 6) examines ERCOT's push toward a 765-kilovolt transmission backbone — the first extra-high-voltage tier in Texas grid history — built specifically to accommodate the surge in data centre, crypto-mining and industrial electricity demand. The approach reverses the usual sequencing: build the transmission corridor first, then let generation and large loads site along it.

Why it matters: Texas's data centre boom has been bottlenecked by grid capacity as much as by land or capital — a wires-first strategy at this scale could decide how fast ERCOT's next wave of AI campuses can actually plug in.

2

Anthropic signs $19B, 20-year lease with TeraWulf for 401MW Kentucky AI data centre

FinanceEnergy

Anthropic has agreed to a 20-year lease worth roughly $19 billion at TeraWulf's Justified Data campus in Hawesville, Kentucky — a 790-acre former aluminum-smelting site with existing power and fiber infrastructure (July 6). The campus will scale to 401 MW of critical IT load, with initial capacity live in late 2027 and full build-out by 2028.

Why it matters: A $19B commitment against roughly $3–4B of TeraWulf's own investment shows AI labs are now underwriting entire power-and-compute campuses directly — reshaping who captures value in the buildout.

3

Alberta greenlights Pembina's 932MW Greenlight gas plant to power new AI data centre

CanadaEnergy

Premier Danielle Smith announced Pembina Pipeline Corp. and partners are proceeding with the Greenlight Electricity Centre, a 932-megawatt natural gas plant in Sturgeon County north of Edmonton, built specifically to supply a proposed AI data centre (July 2). Smith framed it as proof of Alberta's "bring your own power" model, where data centres fund dedicated generation rather than drawing down the public grid.

Why it matters: Alberta is positioning behind-the-meter gas generation as its competitive edge for winning data centre investment — a model other provinces and states are watching closely.

Alberta to review data centre siting rules after Olds backlash, Premier Smith says

CanadaPolicy

After visiting Olds, Alberta and hearing from residents opposed to Synapse Real Estate's proposed 10-building, 1.4-gigawatt gas-and-data-centre complex, Premier Danielle Smith said the province will review guidance on how close data centres can be sited to homes (July 6). The Alberta Utilities Commission has already rejected Synapse's initial application once and paused its resubmission pending more information, while nearly 1,200 residents have registered as interveners.

Why it matters: When a premier who has championed data centre investment starts talking about setback rules, it signals Alberta's fastest-growing project pipeline now faces real siting constraints, not just paperwork delays.

6

US heatwave exposes grid and water strain from AI data centre power demand

EnergyPolicy

A severe heatwave across the US is straining power grids and water supplies at the same time AI data centres are drawing record electricity, with data centres now at roughly 4% of US power demand and projected to reach 9% by 2030 (July 3). Utilities are already asking some data centres to switch to backup generators during peak demand, while roughly 70% of Americans say they oppose new local data centre construction.

Why it matters: Extreme weather is compressing the timeline on a problem the industry hoped to solve gradually — grid and water systems built for "average" conditions are being tested by climate extremes and AI load growth at once.

EnergyPolicy

Finance

Data centre operator Switch, majority-owned by DigitalBridge, is raising roughly $2 billion in a round led by Andreessen Horowitz (committing $400 million), seeking a valuation near $50 billion including debt — up sharply from the $11 billion valuation when investors acquired it in 2022 (July 2). 11

New York's first-in-nation data centre moratorium still awaits Gov. Hochul's signature

Policy

The Responsible Data Center Development Act, which would impose a one-year moratorium on new permits for data centres above 20 MW, passed both houses of New York's legislature on June 4 and remains on Gov. Kathy Hochul's desk with no decision announced as of this week. If signed, it would be the first statewide data centre moratorium in the US.

Why it matters: Hochul is weighing economic-development goals against local and environmental opposition ahead of her re-election bid — her decision will signal how every other state legislature considering similar bills should read the politics.
