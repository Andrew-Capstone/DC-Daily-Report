1

Oracle's credit downgrade collides with Wisconsin's new data-centre credit rules

FinancePolicy

S&P Global's July 9 downgrade of Oracle to BBB- — citing "an uncertain path to profitability" amid a $250 billion AI data-centre buildout that burned nearly $24 billion in free cash flow last fiscal year — has collided with a Wisconsin Public Service Commission rule requiring data-centre customers of We Energies to hold a AAA- credit rating or post steep collateral, potentially over $100 million a year. Oracle now falls below that threshold for its Ozaukee County project; We Energies calls the rule an unfair reading of Oracle's finances and has pushed back, while the PSC has declined to reconsider and consumer group Citizens Utility Board is defending the ratepayer protections.

Why it matters: A state utility regulator using credit ratings to force AI hyperscalers to self-insure against their own default risk — rather than leaving local ratepayers exposed — could become the template other states copy as the sector's debt load climbs toward $570 billion.

2

Oracle's $165 billion "Project Jupiter" hits a second New Mexico rejection, clouding Bloom Energy's fuel-cell bet

EnergyFinance

New Mexico regulators rejected Energy Transfer's natural-gas pipeline application for the second time on public-interest grounds, dealing a fresh blow to Oracle's $165 billion, 2.45-gigawatt "Project Jupiter" AI campus, which had already dropped its original gas-plant plan under local pressure over emissions and water use in favor of Bloom Energy solid-oxide fuel cells. That fuel-cell fallback still needs an air permit that remains pending, leaving the project's power source unresolved on two fronts at once. Bloom Energy shares, up 149% year-to-date, now sit roughly 39% below their June peak as investors reassess how quickly the deal can actually close.

Why it matters: When both the gas-pipeline and fuel-cell paths to power a $165 billion campus stall in the same state within months of each other, it is a sharp reminder that regulatory approval, not capital or chips, is now the binding constraint on hyperscale AI buildouts.

3

Alberta opens comment on "bridging" power framework to fast-track data centres onto the grid

CanadaEnergyPolicy

The Alberta Electric System Operator closed public feedback July 13 on a draft "bring-your-own-generation" bridging framework that would grant qualifying data centres up to three years of temporary grid access while their own on-site power comes online, extending well beyond the 1.6-gigawatt temporary pool the province opened for gas-fired projects earlier this month. A finalized version is expected to roll out in August, building on Phase 1 rules that already allocated 1.2 gigawatts of interim capacity to two projects.

Why it matters: Alberta is trying to codify, province-wide, the exact bring-your-own-power bargain the rest of North America is still fighting over case by case — a durable regulatory template could make it the continent's fastest place to get a hyperscale data centre connected.

4

Off-grid, gas-fired data centre proposed for Letcher County, Kentucky's coalfields

EnergyConstruction

The Appalachian Industrial Development Authority has signed a deal with Diversified Energy and local developer Maverick Holdings to explore purchasing roughly 100 acres at the Gateway Business Park in Jenkins, Kentucky, where Diversified is evaluating a 100-megawatt natural-gas power plant built specifically to run an off-grid AI data centre, WKYT and DCD reported this week. The 260-acre site sits on a 69kV transmission line and is also connected to the gas network via Kentucky Frontier Gas, though no tenant has yet been identified and the project remains in early planning.

Why it matters: A former coal region betting on its existing gas and transmission infrastructure to leapfrog straight to off-grid AI data-centre generation shows how the power-first, tenant-later model is spreading from Texas into Appalachia.

5

Pocatello, Idaho council upholds denial of $2.6 billion data centre after lopsided hearing

Policy

Pocatello's City Council voted 4-1 to uphold a hearing examiner's May decision denying Lex Developments a conditional-use permit for a seven-building, $2.6 billion AI data-centre campus at the former Hoku Materials polysilicon plant, following a July 16 hearing at which only 4 of 90 speakers testified in favor, DCD and local outlets reported. The hearing examiner had found the developer failed to prove the project wouldn't harm community health, safety or welfare; the applicant can still appeal further.

Why it matters: A near-unanimous council vote against a multibillion-dollar project, backed by overwhelming public testimony, is becoming a template other small cities are watching as they weigh their own pending data-centre applications.

6

Piq Energy raises $5 million to help utilities clear North America's grid-connection backlog

TechEnergy

San Francisco-based Piq Energy raised an oversubscribed $5 million seed round led by Active Impact Investments, with Cisco Foundation and several energy-focused funds participating, DCD reported. Piq's agentic grid-planning platform sits on top of utilities' existing engineering software rather than replacing it, aiming to speed up the interconnection studies that are the biggest bottleneck for both new generation and new data-centre loads trying to reach the grid.

Why it matters: With gas turbines backlogged for years and transformers on multi-year lead times, software that shortens the interconnection queue itself attacks a bottleneck that new hardware alone can't fix.
