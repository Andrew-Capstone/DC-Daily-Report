1

AI startup Reflection signs $1 billion capacity agreement with Nebius

TechFinance

AI startup Reflection has agreed to lease more than $1 billion of compute capacity from neocloud provider Nebius, Data Center Dynamics reported July 14, giving Reflection access to Nvidia GPUs through Nebius' cloud platform to train and serve its models. The deal is one of the largest capacity commitments yet from a foundation-model startup to a neocloud rather than a hyperscaler.

Why it matters: Billion-dollar capacity leases between AI labs and neoclouds are becoming the default way new model developers secure GPUs without building their own data centres, reinforcing neoclouds' role as critical infrastructure in the AI supply chain.

Dell shares jump 7.3% on AI server backlog and liquid-cooling edge

TechFinance

Dell Technologies stock rose 7.3% on July 14 after the company disclosed a major expansion of its AI server backlog, driven by new high-value infrastructure contracts with hyperscale providers, according to a TradingKey market note. Analysts pointed to Dell's proprietary liquid-cooling technology as a differentiator as rack power density climbs, with the company's Infrastructure Solutions Group shifting toward higher-margin, AI-optimized configurations.

Why it matters: A server vendor's stock move on backlog growth is a real-time market signal that enterprise AI deployment is moving from pilot projects to large-scale rollouts, with cooling technology now a competitive differentiator rather than an afterthought.

3

Data Center Insights 2026 confronts AI rack limits on cooling, 800VDC power and fiber

Tech

At the Data Center Insights 2026 conference running July 15-16, engineers from Nvidia and Schneider Electric debated 800VDC power architecture for megawatt-scale racks, while other sessions covered liquid-cooling chemistry and the fiber choice between 16-fiber VSFF and connector workarounds for 800G/1.6T networking, Tech Times reported. AI racks already draw 120-150kW and are projected to exceed 1MW by 2027-2028, with 29 hardware vendors now building 800VDC-compatible products.

Why it matters: The industry is converging on 800VDC power distribution as the likely successor to legacy AC architecture, and the standard a data centre picks now will determine whether it can be upgraded for next-generation racks without a rebuild.

4

Hamilton council set to vote today on what could be Canada's first data centre moratorium

CanadaPolicy

Hamilton city council is scheduled to vote July 15 on final bylaw language for a one-year moratorium on new data centres, after the proposal cleared an earlier procedural vote 15-1, CP24 and CBC reported. A researcher who has tracked the issue says the pause, prompted largely by a harbourfront campus proposal, would likely be the first data centre moratorium passed anywhere in Canada, and other municipalities are watching closely.

Why it matters: A final "yes" would make Hamilton a bellwether for how Canadian cities respond to the AI buildout, giving other municipalities a tested legal template for pausing approvals.

5

New York becomes first US state to halt new hyperscale data centres

PolicyEnergy

Governor Kathy Hochul signed an executive order July 14 creating the nation's first statewide moratorium on new hyperscale data centres, directing the Department of Environmental Conservation to withhold discretionary permits for facilities of at least 50MW that hadn't already been deemed complete. The one-year pause tasks the Department of Public Service and Empire State Development with building a Community Investment Framework and new standards for energy, water and air-quality impacts, and Hochul is also pursuing legislation to repeal the state's sales-tax exemption for data centres.

Why it matters: As the first state to formally halt new hyperscale development rather than just study it, New York's move gives every other statehouse weighing a moratorium bill a working precedent — and signals to developers that even major tech-forward states now see political upside in slowing the AI buildout.

6

Jackson becomes first Mississippi city to pass a data centre moratorium

Policy

The Jackson City Council approved a 183-day moratorium on data centre permits and rezoning applications by a 5-2 vote July 14, Mississippi Today reported, with the non-unanimous margin meaning the pause takes effect in 30 days rather than immediately. During the freeze the city plans to identify appropriate sites for data centres and study the impact of development on its already-strained water system.

Why it matters: Jackson's fragile water infrastructure makes it a sharper test than most of whether a mid-sized Southern city can get ahead of a hyperscale project before it's approved, rather than fighting it after the fact.

7

Leon County, Texas rejects Crusoe Technologies' tax break bid for $34 billion data centre

PolicyFinance

Leon County commissioners rejected Crusoe Technologies' initial tax abatement application for a proposed $34 billion data centre on procedural grounds, citing an incomplete submission, KXXV reported, after more than 200 residents packed the meeting in opposition. Crusoe is expected to refile a complete application, with a public hearing now set for July 27; the facility is projected to come online in 2028.

Why it matters: Even a technical rejection on paperwork grounds, backed by heavy turnout, shows rural Texas counties are no longer rubber-stamping abatements for mega-projects the way they did earlier in the AI buildout.

8

Palm Beach County commissioners to decide fate of "Project Tango" AI data centre

Policy

Palm Beach County commissioners are set to take a final vote July 15 on PBA Holdings' "Project Tango," a 1.5-million-square-foot data centre near the Arden community and Saddle View Elementary School, after the county's Zoning Board unanimously recommended denial following a nearly five-hour hearing, CBS12 reported. More than 40 residents spoke against the project, with debate centering on whether it should be classified as light or heavy industrial use.

Why it matters: A denial would be another sign that even fast-growing Sun Belt counties are drawing a line on data centres sited near schools and homes, regardless of the classification loopholes developers try to use.

9

St. Charles County, Missouri approves six-month data centre moratorium

Policy

The St. Charles County Council voted unanimously July 14 to approve a six-month moratorium on new data centre projects, St. Louis Public Radio reported, with County Executive Steve Ehlmann saying growth "cannot come at the expense" of residents. The county plans to bring in outside experts for an impact study during the pause, and is looking at neighboring St. Charles City's stricter rules as a possible model.

Why it matters: Missouri has become one of the most contested data centre battlegrounds in the country this month, and a unanimous county-level pause adds to a fast-building patchwork of local rules that developers now have to navigate project by project.

10

Stokes County, North Carolina rejects its own data centre moratorium as "Project Delta" looms

Policy

Stokes County commissioners voted July 14 against a proposed six-month moratorium on new data centre proposals, WFMY reported, despite the planning board's recommendation for a pause and continued community opposition to "Project Delta," a large facility planned near Walnut Cove. The vote comes after commissioners were forced to void their own January rezoning approval for the project over a notice-timing error, meaning the developer must now restart the approval process from scratch.

Why it matters: Stokes County's rejection is a rare counter-example to the moratorium wave sweeping other jurisdictions, showing rural counties courting the tax base and jobs a project like Project Delta promises aren't uniformly following the pause trend.

11

St. Joseph, Missouri moves to stop accepting new data centre applications

Policy

St. Joseph's City Council agreed to halt new data centre applications and seek outside expertise to rewrite the city's zoning code, the News-Press NOW reported, after months of resident concern and the withdrawal of a rezoning request for a proposed facility on Pickett Road. The city has since cancelled a planned July 16 town hall and pulled the rezoning item from the Planning Commission's July 23 agenda.

Why it matters: A developer's application withdrawal following sustained local pressure, rather than a forced denial, shows how community opposition is increasingly getting projects pulled before they even reach a formal vote.

Prime Data Centers shelves $500 million Nordic bond sale amid weak demand

Finance

Prime Data Centers postponed a planned bond sale under Norwegian law, Bloomberg reported, after downsizing the offering from $600 million to $500 million and still finding some investors unwilling to participate under the proposed terms. Proceeds were meant to fund existing and future data centre projects for the US-based operator.

Why it matters: A stalled bond sale is an early sign that investor appetite for AI-exposed debt has limits, adding weight to warnings that uneven underwriting standards across the sector's roughly $5.8 trillion projected buildout could leave some bondholders exposed.

14

Stonebridge pitches $15 billion, 16-building data centre campus on former Pennsylvania dynamite factory site

Construction

Maryland-based developer Stonebridge Associates is proposing a 16-building data centre campus and electrical substation on the 569-acre Powder Mills site in Lawrence County, Pennsylvania — former home to an American Cyanamid plant that manufactured nitroglycerine-based explosives until 1972 — with more than $15 billion in planned investment, Data Center Dynamics reported. The project could generate 3,000-4,000 construction and permanent jobs and take up to six years to build.

Why it matters: Repurposing a shuttered industrial explosives site for hyperscale computing is a striking example of how developers are chasing large, already-disturbed parcels with existing infrastructure to sidestep the greenfield zoning fights slowing projects elsewhere.
