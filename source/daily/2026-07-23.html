1

OpenAI reveals 3.2GW "Project Camellia" campus in Effingham County, Georgia

ConstructionFinanceTech

OpenAI unveiled Project Camellia, a 3.2-gigawatt data centre campus in the Savannah Gateway Industrial Hub near Savannah, Georgia — the first facility OpenAI is designing and building itself. The company is committing $20 billion to qualify for local incentives (with total costs likely exceeding $30 billion), contracting Georgia Power for phased delivery of the full 3.2GW between 2028 and 2032, and pledging $80 million in community benefits plus a closed-loop cooling system.

Why it matters: OpenAI moving from tenant to self-designed developer signals frontier AI labs now see owning gigawatt-scale infrastructure directly as a competitive necessity, not just a leased convenience.

AMD to invest up to $5B in Anthropic as it commits to 2GW of Instinct MI450 GPUs

TechFinance

AMD and Anthropic announced a strategic partnership under which Anthropic will deploy up to 2 gigawatts of AMD Instinct MI450 GPUs in AMD's liquid-cooled Helios racks, starting with the first gigawatt in the first half of 2027. AMD is committing up to $5 billion in milestone-linked equity investment, while Anthropic will use Claude to help optimize AMD's ROCm software stack and AMD adopts Claude across its own engineering teams.

Why it matters: A leading AI lab locking in multi-gigawatt AMD capacity, backed by AMD's own capital, is the clearest sign yet that the industry wants a credible alternative to Nvidia's compute monopoly.

3

MGX, AIP and BlackRock's GIP complete $40B acquisition of Aligned Data Centers

Finance

A consortium of Abu Dhabi's MGX, the AI Infrastructure Partnership and BlackRock's Global Infrastructure Partners closed its $40 billion acquisition of Aligned Data Centers from Macquarie Asset Management, the largest M&A deal the data centre sector has ever recorded. The consortium committed a further $5 billion for Aligned's expansion; the company's 51 campuses and 6.4GW of operational and planned capacity across North and South America will keep CEO Andrew Schaap and stay headquartered in Dallas.

Why it matters: Sovereign wealth and infrastructure capital just set a new high-water mark for data centre M&A, underscoring how much of the AI buildout is now being financed by global institutional money rather than tech balance sheets.

4

Idaho National Laboratory, Nvidia, AWS and X-energy launch Project Prometheus to halve nuclear build times

EnergyTech

Idaho National Laboratory launched Project Prometheus with Nvidia, AWS and SMR developer X-energy, a three-year, $60 million initiative under the Department of Energy's Genesis Mission — the largest grant among all selected projects. The effort will use AI across design, licensing, manufacturing and operation of reactors, targeting at least a 2x acceleration in deployment schedules and over 50% cuts to operating costs; X-energy is contributing $10 million and its Xe-100 SMR and TRISO-X fuel data.

Why it matters: Pairing AI directly with nuclear licensing and construction is aimed at the single biggest bottleneck in getting reactors online fast enough to feed gigawatt-scale AI campuses.

5

White House warns PJM to reform grid governance "before it is too late"

PolicyEnergy

The White House escalated pressure on PJM Interconnection, the largest US grid operator, warning that it has failed to act on a January Statement of Principles co-signed with all 13 PJM state governors calling for capacity-market reform, faster generation buildout and a governance overhaul of PJM's industry-controlled board. The administration wants data centre and other large-load customers to bear more of the cost of the capacity they add to the grid, ahead of PJM's May 2027 capacity auction.

Why it matters: A rare bipartisan White House-and-governors coalition pressuring the nation's largest grid operator shows data-centre-driven electricity costs have become a top-tier political issue, not just a utility planning problem.

6

US data centres could consume up to 194GW of power by 2035, BNEF forecast jumps 83%

EnergyPolicy

BloombergNEF nearly doubled its long-range US data-centre power forecast, now projecting demand could hit 194 gigawatts by 2035 — an 83% jump from its December estimate of 106GW — driven by projects like Microsoft's 2GW Pecos, Texas facility and Meta's up to 5GW Louisiana campus. At that level, data centres would consume roughly 20% of total US electricity, up from 5.9% today, with analysts warning of a possible 19GW generation shortfall even at record buildout rates.

Why it matters: A forecast revision this large in just seven months shows planners are still chronically underestimating how fast AI power demand is growing, which is exactly the dynamic straining grids like PJM and ERCOT today.

EnergyConstruction

TechFinance

Italian cable maker Prysmian signed a €5.5 billion ($6.29 billion) agreement with Koch-owned Molex to supply optical cables for AI data centres over as long as ten years, including a €550 million upfront payment. Why it matters: A multibillion-dollar, decade-long supply lock-in for optical interconnects shows fibre and cabling capacity is becoming as strategically contested as chips and power in the AI buildout.

TechConstruction

Nvidia supplier Wistron opened its first US manufacturing facility, a $700 million, 324,000-square-foot smart factory in Fort Worth, producing Nvidia's GB300 Grace Blackwell Ultra superchips and soon the Vera Rubin superchip. 11

Data centre-related companies account for record 14.4% of new US industrial leasing

FinanceConstruction

A Cushman & Wakefield analysis found data centre-related companies now account for a record 14.4% of all new industrial leasing across the US, up 44% year-over-year, across six major markets including Virginia, Atlanta, Chicago, Phoenix, Austin and Dallas-Fort Worth. The firm estimates the six markets hold 19.5GW of capacity with 11.7GW under construction and 84.9GW more planned, generating an estimated 81,000-124,000 industrial and downstream jobs.

Why it matters: Data centres are now reshaping the broader industrial real estate market, not just a niche subsector, crowding out warehouse and logistics tenants in the country's hottest markets.

Could a New York-style data centre moratorium happen in Canada? Every province says not yet

CanadaPolicy

Days after New York became the first US state to pause new hyperscale data-centre permits, CBC News asked every Canadian province and territory whether they would consider a similar moratorium — none committed to one. The question comes just after Hamilton, Ontario's city council voted down its own proposed one-year data-centre moratorium, even as companies keep eyeing Canada for new hyperscale buildouts and local pushback grows over grid strain and water use.

Why it matters: With no province willing to follow New York's lead, Canada's data-centre pipeline — already projected at over 22 gigawatts in planning — looks set to keep expanding largely unchecked by provincial policy, at least for now.
