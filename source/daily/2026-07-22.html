1

Cleco to build 756MW gas plant for Applied Digital's $3.6B Louisiana campus

EnergyConstruction

Louisiana utility Cleco plans to construct a 756-megawatt natural-gas plant to serve Applied Digital's $3.6 billion Delta Forge 1 data centre in Rapides Parish, the largest single load Cleco has ever served. Applied will cover the full ten-year power-supply agreement and infrastructure costs, while Cleco separately plans to buy 370MW from Texas's Tenaska Frontier plant; construction is set to employ about 1,000 workers with initial operations targeted for mid-2027.

Why it matters: Another utility is shifting the cost and construction burden of new gas generation entirely onto a single hyperscale tenant — a financing pattern quickly becoming the default way US data centres get built power.

2

Microsoft to deploy AMD's open-standard Helios racks across Azure

Tech

Microsoft will deploy AMD's Helios rackscale solution — a liquid-cooled design packing 72 Instinct MI455X GPUs, sixth-gen EPYC "Venice" CPUs and Pensando networking into one rack — across Azure starting in the second half of 2026. New Azure ND MI455X v7 GPU instances and two CPU-only VM families will target frontier-model inference and data-heavy AI pipelines.

Why it matters: A top-three hyperscaler committing to an open-standard AMD rack for production inference is the clearest signal yet that buyers want an alternative to Nvidia's proprietary infrastructure stack.

3

Crusoe and ON.energy to deploy 5GW of grid-isolating AI UPS systems

TechEnergy

Crusoe has partnered with ON.energy to deploy 5 gigawatts of medium-voltage AI UPS systems across its hyperscale campuses, with commissioning starting this year and continuing into 2027. The technology sits between power sources and the data centre, decoupling facilities from the grid so they can ride through voltage faults and absorb the synchronized power swings of thousands of GPUs ramping training workloads up and down; it has been validated against ERCOT's large-load interconnection rules.

Why it matters: AI training's erratic power draw is becoming as much a grid-engineering problem as a generation problem, and this is one of the largest deployments yet of hardware built specifically to keep that volatility off the public grid.

New Mexico regulators reject gas pipeline for Oracle's 2.5GW Project Jupiter

EnergyPolicy

New Mexico regulators rejected a proposed natural-gas pipeline that would have supplied power generation for Oracle's planned 2.5-gigawatt Project Jupiter data centre, one of the largest AI campuses proposed in the US Southwest. The decision leaves the project's power plan without its intended fuel source.

Why it matters: A state regulator blocking gas infrastructure for a gigawatt-scale AI project shows permitting risk now cuts both ways — communities are stopping generation buildouts, not just the data centres themselves.

5

DOE picks Amentum, DC Blox for 1GW AI campus at Savannah River site

EnergyPolicy

The Department of Energy's National Nuclear Security Administration selected Amentum and Atlanta-based DC Blox to develop a 1-gigawatt AI data centre backed by roughly 2 gigawatts of dedicated on-site power generation at the federally owned Savannah River site in Aiken County, South Carolina. Power generation would start on natural gas and later shift to nuclear; the companies now enter lease negotiations, part of a broader federal push to build off-grid AI data centres on DOE land at four sites including Idaho National Laboratory and Oak Ridge.

Why it matters: Federal land is emerging as a fast-track path around the local permitting fights slowing private projects elsewhere, and pairing it with a gas-to-nuclear power roadmap previews how Washington wants future AI campuses built.

6

Canadian miner-turned-AI firm Hut 8 signs second $9.8B Texas lease, fully commercializes 1GW campus

CanadaFinanceEnergy

Hut 8, the Toronto-founded, Miami-headquartered bitcoin-miner-turned-AI infrastructure company, signed a second 15-year, $9.8 billion lease with an unnamed high-investment-grade tenant, doubling that customer's contracted capacity to 704MW and fully commercializing the 1-gigawatt Beacon Point campus near Corpus Christi, Texas. The campus, built on Nvidia's DSX reference architecture for gigawatt-scale AI factories, now carries a base contract value of $19.6 billion over 15 years, rising to as much as $50.2 billion if renewal options are exercised; initial energization is on schedule for Q1 2027.

Why it matters: A Canadian-founded operator just locked in one of the largest data-centre lease books in the industry, underscoring how much Canadian capital and expertise sits behind the US AI buildout even as Canada's own domestic capacity lags.

7

Telus advances three new AI data centres in British Columbia

CanadaConstruction

Telus is developing three AI data centres in BC under the federal government's Enabling Large-Scale Sovereign AI Data Centres initiative: an expansion of an existing Kamloops facility going online later this year, a new six-storey Mount Pleasant, Vancouver site launching late 2026 for 2028 completion, and a Yaletown facility near BC Place tied into Creative Energy's steam plant. CEO Darren Entwistle cited sellout demand at Telus's first AI factory in Rimouski, Quebec, as proof of Canadian appetite for domestic AI infrastructure.

Why it matters: With Alberta absorbing the bulk of Canada's hyperscale pipeline, Telus's BC push shows domestic "sovereign AI" capacity is also expanding in dense urban cores, not just on cheap rural land.

8

Canada's planned data-centre capacity hits 22.2GW as opposition builds nationwide

CanadaPolicy

Canada had 194 active data-centre facilities with a combined 1.6GW of capacity as of June 2026, alongside another 213 projects totalling 22.2GW in planning or development — with Alberta accounting for 92% of that planned capacity despite holding just 3.3% of active capacity today, thanks to its deregulated market and abundant on-site generation options. Community opposition is building in parallel as the new hyperscale facilities, mostly sited in rural areas, become a flashpoint for anxieties about AI.

Why it matters: The scale of Canada's pipeline — and how lopsidedly it favours Alberta — will shape which provinces capture AI investment and which absorb the community backlash already visible in Ontario and BC.

9

IREN signs $2.8B in AI cloud deals built on customer prepayments

Finance

IREN signed $2.8 billion in new multiyear AI cloud contracts, lifting its year-end annualized run-rate revenue target from $3.7 billion to more than $4 billion, with roughly 85% of that target now under contract. New customer prepayments cover about 45% of the GPU capex tied to the deployments, cutting IREN's own funding needs; its customer roster now includes Microsoft, Nvidia, Perplexity and Figure AI, with capacity expanding from 3MW a year ago to 480MW this year and 1.2GW targeted for 2027.

Why it matters: Customers funding GPU purchases upfront is a meaningful shift in how neocloud capacity gets financed, easing the capital burden that has made investors nervous about the sector's debt load.

11

AI data-centre boom leaves construction industry the most capacity-constrained sector globally

Construction

Turner & Townsend's Global Construction Market Intelligence 2026 report, covering 112 markets in 44 countries, found more than 70% of markets report data-centre contractor capacity as tightening or overstretched, with nearly 90% citing shortages in mechanical, electrical and plumbing trades. The report ties the strain to roughly $700 billion in hyperscale capex this year and points to modular, factory-built construction as the industry's main pressure valve.

Why it matters: Skilled-trade shortages, not permitting or power alone, are becoming a hard ceiling on how fast the industry can physically build — a constraint no amount of capital can immediately fix.

Pasco, Washington council unanimously approves six-month data-centre moratorium

Policy

The Pasco City Council voted 6-0 to immediately pause new development permits for data centres, blockchain, AI computing and crypto-mining facilities for six months, giving the city time to draft data-centre-specific land-use rules. A public hearing is required within 60 days; the proposal followed resident concerns over energy, land and cooling-infrastructure impacts.

Why it matters: A unanimous vote — not a split one — shows moratorium politics have moved from controversial to close to consensus in some municipalities.

Edgerton, Kansas council overrides planning commission to let $700M Damac campus proceed

PolicyConstruction

17

Western Virginia Water Authority pushes back on misinformation over Google data-centre water use

Policy

The Western Virginia Water Authority said its signed agreement caps a proposed Google data centre at 2 million gallons of water per day initially — far below the 10-11 million gallons circulating in community rumours — with room to grow to 8 million gallons only if existing customer needs are still met. The authority, which treats 21 million gallons daily and has handled up to 23 million in the past, said it is confident it can serve both Google and its current customers.

Why it matters: As water use becomes a leading flashpoint for data-centre opposition, this is a rare case of a public utility publishing hard numbers to counter viral claims rather than letting the narrative go unanswered.

18

Virginia's first-in-the-nation data-centre power tax takes effect

PolicyFinance

Virginia's new $0.011-per-kilowatt-hour electricity consumption tax on data centres took effect July 1, applying to utility-supplied and self-generated power alike, with first payments due in September. The tax is projected to raise about $600 million annually for the state's general fund and sunsets in mid-2028.

Why it matters: As the country's largest data-centre market, Virginia's move to directly tax facility power use — rather than just offering incentives — gives other states a concrete template for capturing revenue from the AI buildout.
