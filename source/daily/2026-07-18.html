1

Nvidia's Rubin-generation data centres achieve 100% liquid cooling, near-zero water use

TechEnergy

Nvidia says its Rubin-generation AI architecture is the first to cool every chip and networking component entirely by liquid in a closed loop with no fans, running coolant at 45°C (113°F) to passively reject heat. The design uses a 75% water, 25% propylene-glycol coolant mix, cuts data-centre water use from roughly 2.6 million gallons per megawatt annually to near zero, and lets systems fit in 2 rack units instead of 6, with mass production and shipments beginning this autumn.

Why it matters: A 100%-liquid-cooled reference design that all but eliminates data-centre water consumption tackles the industry's most politically explosive resource fight just as communities nationwide cite water use to block new projects.

2

Meta breaks ground on $13 billion Sturgeon County, Alberta data centre — its first in Canada

CanadaEnergyConstruction

Meta officially broke ground on a CAD $13 billion, 1-gigawatt AI campus in Sturgeon County, Alberta on July 8 — its first Canadian data centre and 33rd worldwide — with Premier Danielle Smith and Meta data-centre VP Gary Demasi at the ceremony. The project is expected to employ up to 3,000 workers at peak construction, generate roughly $250 million a year in royalties, taxes and fees for Alberta, and will use a closed-loop, dry-cooling system with no operational water draw, matched with 100% clean and renewable electricity.

Why it matters: The largest private-sector investment of its kind in Canadian history, paired with a zero-operational-water cooling commitment, sets the template Alberta and other provinces will use to court the next wave of hyperscale investment.

3

Aalo Atomics' test reactor reaches criticality, fourth DOE-backed microreactor to hit July 4 goal

EnergyTech

Aalo Atomics' 10-MWe sodium-cooled Aalo-X Critical Test Reactor reached criticality at Idaho National Laboratory on July 4, becoming the fourth advanced reactor to do so under the Department of Energy's Reactor Pilot Program deadline, Business Wire reported. The test reactor shares full-scale core components with Aalo's commercial "Aalo Pod" design, which the Austin-based company is positioning as a power source built specifically for on-site data-centre deployment.

Why it matters: A working microreactor design purpose-built to co-locate with data centres shows the nuclear-for-AI thesis moving from paper agreements to physical hardware years ahead of the utility-scale SMRs hyperscalers have already committed billions to.

Meta's in-house "Iris" AI chip enters production in September as capacity doubles to 14GW

TechFinance

Meta will begin manufacturing its custom MTIA "Iris" AI processor in September after it cleared testing in six weeks with no major issues, MLQ reported, citing an internal memo. Broadcom is Meta's design partner through 2029 and TSMC will fabricate the chip, which is meant to reduce Meta's reliance on Nvidia and AMD as the company doubles its AI computing capacity from 7 gigawatts in 2026 to 14 gigawatts in 2027.

Why it matters: A hyperscaler moving custom silicon from testing to production on a six-month cadence shows in-house chips are becoming a core part of how data-centre capacity gets built, not just a hedge against Nvidia supply.

5

Samsung Heavy Industries targets 2028 for floating, barge-based AI data centre

TechEnergy

Samsung Heavy Industries is developing a purpose-built 50MW barge data centre — combining server halls, onboard power generation and LNG fuel storage — with an initial "nearshore" version drawing partly on land-based grid power before fully offshore deployment, TheNextWeb reported July 8. The approach is designed to sidestep three converging constraints: land scarcity, multi-year grid interconnection queues, and freshwater cooling demand, using seawater for cooling instead.

Why it matters: A commercial timeline for offshore compute shows land and grid bottlenecks are pushing the industry toward genuinely novel siting models, not just further out into rural counties.

6

Hamilton, Ontario council rejects one-year data centre moratorium 10-6

CanadaPolicy

Hamilton city council voted 10-6 on July 15 to reject an interim control bylaw that would have paused new data-centre development for a year, CBC reported, after a closed-door legal briefing warned a McMaster University-linked project would likely have been exempt anyway and that the bylaw risked costly legal challenges. The proposal, which would have made Hamilton the first Canadian city to pass such a pause, had cleared an earlier planning-committee vote 15-1; councillor Nrinder Nann said roughly ten other Canadian municipalities had requested copies of her motion.

Why it matters: Killing the bylaw that other Canadian cities were waiting to copy shows how quickly a legal technicality and investment-jitters can unwind momentum that looked unstoppable just a day earlier.

7

New York becomes first US state to impose a statewide data centre moratorium

PolicyEnergy

Governor Kathy Hochul signed an executive order on July 14 pausing state environmental permits for new hyperscale data centres for up to a year while New York develops a Generic Environmental Impact Statement and stronger regulatory standards, according to the governor's office. The order also directs a Community Investment Framework within 60 days for local benefit negotiations, floats a New York Grid Acceleration Fund, and pursues legislation to repeal the state's sales-tax exemption for large data centres.

Why it matters: The first statewide hyperscale moratorium in the country hands every other state legislature a ready-made template for pausing permits while they write their own rules.

PJM finds data centres added $23 billion to public electricity bills through 2028

EnergyPolicy

PJM Interconnection, which oversees electricity markets across 14 mid-Atlantic and Midwest states, concluded that expected power demand from data centres was a primary driver of $23 billion in customer price increases that will persist through at least the end of 2028, Fortune reported July 14. The finding intensifies debate over how the cost of new substations and transmission upgrades needed for AI data centres should be allocated between residential, commercial and industrial ratepayers.

Why it matters: A regulator putting a specific $23 billion price tag on data-centre-driven bill increases turns an abstract "who pays for the grid" argument into a number state legislators and utility commissions can no longer avoid.

15

Monterey Park becomes first US city to ban data centres by ballot measure

Policy

Monterey Park, California voters passed Measure NDC on June 2 by an overwhelming margin, permanently banning data centres citywide; the result was certified in early July after the standard 30-day canvass, per ABC7 and Ballotpedia. The measure was triggered by a proposal to convert a former shopping mall into a 250,000-square-foot, roughly 50MW data centre that opponents said would have tripled the city's total electricity use.

Why it matters: The first voter-enacted municipal data-centre ban in the country gives opposition groups elsewhere a proven, court-tested playbook beyond council votes and zoning fights.

Study: data centre opponents blocked or delayed $130 billion in projects in Q1 2026 alone

Policy

Data Center Watch, a project of AI research firm 10a Labs, found that opponents blocked or delayed at least 75 data-centre projects worth roughly $130 billion from January through March 2026 — matching all of 2025's total in a single quarter — NBC News reported. The number of active opposition groups more than doubled, from 396 at the end of 2025 to 833 by March, with Maryland, Ohio and Texas leading in group counts.

Why it matters: Researchers call this a structural shift rather than a cyclical spike, meaning developers should expect organized resistance as a standing cost of doing business rather than a passing phase.

Policy

Fort Worth's zoning commission voted 7-4 on July 8 to deny a proposed ordinance that would have restricted data centres to industrial zones, required 250-foot setbacks from homes and banned cryptocurrency mining, with commissioners citing insufficient detail, the Fort Worth Report said.
