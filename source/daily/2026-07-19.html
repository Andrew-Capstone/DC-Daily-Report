1

SpaceX quietly rents Colossus GPU capacity to Anthropic and Google for ~$26 billion a year, eyes Pentagon deal

TechFinance

SpaceX is renting spare Nvidia GPU capacity from its Memphis-area Colossus and Colossus II data centres to Anthropic (325,000 GPUs for $1.25 billion a month) and Google (110,000 GPUs for $920 million a month), Fortune reported July 19 — a combined run rate of roughly $26 billion a year. The company is separately in talks to supply the Pentagon with AI data-centre capacity potentially worth billions, following a $3.2 billion AI-segment 2025 revenue base that still ran a $6.4 billion operating loss.

Why it matters: A rocket company turning idle AI compute into a business line rivaling a mid-size cloud provider — with the Pentagon as a prospective customer — shows how thoroughly GPU capacity itself has become the asset class investors are pricing SpaceX's $1.8 trillion valuation against.

2

Elon Musk quietly acquired mobile gas-turbine firm APR Energy for over $1 billion to power xAI

EnergyFinance

Musk acquired Jacksonville-based APR Energy — which operates trailer-mounted gas and diesel turbines capable of generating more than 1 gigawatt and deploying in 15-30 days — in a deal worth over $1 billion completed quietly in May 2026, DCD and Tom's Hardware reported, only surfacing publicly this month via an FTC early-termination notice. The acquisition gives xAI a mobile, grid-independent power fleet it can deploy directly at data-centre sites without waiting on interconnection queues.

Why it matters: Buying an entire mobile power-generation company rather than just contracting for turbines shows Musk treating on-site generation as core infrastructure to own outright, intensifying the gas-turbine emissions fight already surrounding his Memphis Colossus sites.

3

Dominion Energy pilots LōD's grid-responsive AI inference technology in Ashland, Virginia

TechEnergy

Startup LōD was selected for Dominion Energy Innovation Center's 2026 Accelerate program and will pilot its CLōD platform in Dominion's PJM territory, letting AI inference workloads shift in real time based on live electricity prices and grid conditions without degrading data-centre performance, DCD reported July 17. The test aims to prove AI infrastructure can act as an active grid participant rather than a fixed, inflexible load.

Why it matters: Software that makes AI inference genuinely grid-responsive — rather than requiring new turbines, batteries or transmission — attacks the interconnection bottleneck from the demand side, inside the world's largest data-centre grid market.

4

QTS and Lancium unveil $10 billion, 1GW, 11-building AI campus in Hall County, Texas

ConstructionEnergy

QTS Data Centers and Lancium announced plans to invest $10 billion building up to 11 data centres across roughly 465 acres near Turkey in Hall County, Texas, anchored by a 1-gigawatt grid connection, DCD reported. Lancium and QTS will fund 100% of the energy infrastructure themselves, including on-site solar and battery storage, and the closed-loop cooling design draws all water from on-site wells rather than the town's municipal supply.

Why it matters: A hyperscale operator and power developer footing the entire grid and water bill upfront, rather than leaning on a small Texas town's utilities, is becoming the template rural counties are starting to demand before they'll say yes.

5

CleanSpark signs $6.6 billion, 20-year data centre lease in Sandersville, Georgia

FinanceEnergy

Bitcoin miner CleanSpark signed a 20-year infrastructure lease covering 175 megawatts of critical IT load with an unnamed "high-investment-grade" global technology company at its Sandersville, Georgia campus, PR Newswire reported July 14, worth roughly $6.6 billion over the initial term ($11.6 billion with extensions). The same tenant also took exclusivity on CleanSpark's entire 885MW Texas portfolio, with production-grade deliveries starting in the fourth quarter of 2027.

Why it matters: A crypto miner locking in a two-decade, multi-billion-dollar hyperscale lease shows how completely the bitcoin-to-AI pivot has reshaped the economics of miners' power-rich real estate.

6

Canadian Press: opposition brewing nationally as Canada's data-centre pipeline nears 20GW

CanadaPolicy

A Canadian Press analysis published July 18 found Canada has 1.6GW of active data-centre capacity against 22.2GW planned or under development as of June — Alberta alone accounting for 92% of the pipeline despite hosting just 3.3% of current facilities — with opposition organizing from Vancouver to Hamilton. A new Leger poll cited in the piece found Canadians split 46% supportive to 37% opposed, while researchers framed data centres as "the physical avatar" of broader public anxiety about AI.

Why it matters: With planned capacity outnumbering active capacity nearly 14-to-1, Canada is entering the same fight the US had last year over siting and power — except concentrated almost entirely in one province's gas-fired grid.

7

Bankers shop stakes in DataBank, EdgeCore and Netrality as data centre operators seek exits

Finance

Bankers are presenting stakes in DataBank (a sale could value it near $25 billion), Denver-based EdgeCore Digital Infrastructure and Netrality Data Centers to private-equity buyers, with KKR's newly launched Helix Digital Infrastructure platform among the interested parties, the Wall Street Journal reported this week. DataBank is currently owned by a DigitalBridge-led consortium including Swiss Life, EDF Invest and AustralianSuper; EdgeCore is owned by Switzerland's Partners Group.

Why it matters: Multiple owners racing to sell majority stakes at once — rather than one-off deals — signals institutional investors want to cash out physical AI infrastructure near the top of the market, even as demand for the sector stays strong.

8

Brookfield-backed Csquare goes public but falls short of share price and valuation target

Finance

Colocation operator Csquare priced its NYSE IPO at $21 a share — below its marketed $23-27 range — raising just over $1 billion against a $1.35 billion target and valuing the company near $3.2 billion, well under the roughly $4.2 billion it could have reached at the top of the range, DCD reported. Shares slipped further on debut, with buyers reportedly wary of the company's debt load and ongoing losses.

Why it matters: A weak reception for a retail-colocation IPO — a lower-risk profile than hyperscale developers — is an early signal that public markets are starting to price AI-infrastructure risk more carefully rather than paying up for any exposure to the theme.

9

Bosch wins $225 million in US CHIPS Act funding for California silicon-carbide fab

TechFinance

Bosch received $225 million from the US CHIPS Program Office to support a silicon-carbide semiconductor fab in Roseville, California, DCD reported July 17. SiC chips are used in power-conversion electronics — including the high-efficiency power systems increasingly deployed in AI data centres — making the fab part of the domestic supply chain feeding data-centre power hardware.

Why it matters: Federal money still flowing to power-semiconductor manufacturing, even amid broader CHIPS Act scrutiny, underscores that the AI buildout's chip bottleneck extends well beyond GPUs into the components that convert and manage power at the rack level.

10

Stream proposes 867-acre, up-to-$10 billion "Project Saltworks" data centre in Graham, Texas

Construction

Dallas-based Stream Data Centers, through affiliate Headwaters Site Development, proposed a 5-to-15-building campus on 867 acres in unincorporated Young County near Graham, Texas, on land it bought from Plug Power, DCD reported July 17. The initial phase alone represents $1-1.5 billion in taxable value — more than Young County's entire current tax base — with the full build-out requiring an estimated $10 billion and construction targeted for early 2027 pending a hyperscale tenant and power scheduling.

Why it matters: A project whose first phase would out-value an entire rural Texas county's tax base illustrates just how disproportionate the fiscal stakes have become for the small counties now fielding hyperscaler land deals.

11

Duos Edge AI signs $111 million, five-year, 10MW capacity deal with an "investment-grade hyperscaler"

Finance

Edge-data-centre operator Duos Edge AI agreed to provide 10 megawatts of capacity over five years to an unnamed investment-grade hyperscale customer in a deal worth $111 million, DCD reported July 17. The agreement extends the smaller-format edge-colocation model into direct hyperscaler contracts, a segment that has increasingly been dominated by much larger campus deals.

Why it matters: A relatively small operator locking in a nine-figure hyperscaler contract shows demand for AI capacity is now deep enough to pull edge-scale providers into deals that used to be reserved for the largest wholesale players.
